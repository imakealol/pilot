CHAPTER 15

THE APPROACH

The road narrows as they climb into the green zone.
Elena keeps her eyes on the cracked asphalt, her hands steady on the wheel. The Rover hums beneath her—fully charged, systems nominal, everything running exactly as it should. The problem isn’t the machine.
The problem is the man in the passenger seat.
Hendricks hasn’t spoken in an hour. He’s watching the landscape change—brown scrub giving way to actual grass, skeletal trees thickening into something that almost looks like forest. The Quiet Zone’s influence bleeds outward. The closer you get, the more the world remembers what it used to be.
In the back seat, Standard is still. Not sleeping—she doesn’t sleep—but somewhere else. That faraway look she gets now, like she’s listening to something no one else can hear.
Elena realizes, with a small shock, that Standard has never asked where they’re going—not once, not at the checkpoint, not at the Array, not at the Dam. She just follows. Goes where Hendricks goes. Like a companion bot would.
Because that’s what she is. Was. The corporate gift Hendricks never opened, sitting in a crate for years until something woke her up.
Elena’s hand drifts to the scar on her palm. The implant hums at the base of her skull, low and constant. 287.3 Hz. Her mother’s frequency.
Tomorrow they reach the checkpoint.
Tomorrow she walks into her father’s compound alongside a man who was going there anyway.

“You’re quiet.”
Hendricks’ voice cuts through the engine noise. Elena glances at him. His face is harder to read now—the aging has changed the architecture of it, deepened the lines, pulled the skin tighter across the bones. Mid-fifties, maybe. He looked forty-five a week ago.
“Long drive,” she says.
“Uh-huh.”
He doesn’t push. That’s the thing about Hendricks; he lets a question sit until you forget you’re the one who has to fill the silence, and then you fill it anyway.
“The checkpoint’s tomorrow,” she says. “We should talk about what happens when we get there.”
“Brother Ash’s people scan everyone. Strip anything electronic. Confess your sins on analog tape.” Hendricks’ voice is flat. “I know the ritual.”
“You’ve been through it before.”
“Once. Fourteen months ago.”
Elena’s grip tightens on the wheel. Fourteen months ago. Before the Miracle. When Hendricks was still NED security, still Morton’s dog, still—
“You were there,” she says. The words come out before she can stop them. “Fourteen months ago. You helped take down the tower.”
“I disabled the failsafes. Someone else led the assault.”
“Why?”
“Morton asked me to.”
The answer is too simple. Too clean. Elena waits for more, but Hendricks just looks out the window.
“That’s it? Morton asked, so you did it?”
“That’s how it worked. For forty years.” He pauses. “I was heading to the Quiet Zone before you found me. Before she woke up.” He tilts his head toward the back seat, toward Standard. “This was always where I was going.”
Elena feels something cold settle in her stomach. “Why?”
“Unfinished business.”
“What kind of—”
“The kind that stays unfinished until it’s finished.”
He’s not going to explain. Elena can see it in the set of his jaw, the way his hands rest too still in his lap. She’s been treating him like cargo, like a piece of her mission, like the route under the seawall with legs, and the whole time he was steering himself toward the same door she was, on his own business, in his own time. He was never hers to deliver.
He was going anyway.
She just caught a ride.
And he’s being honest with her. More honest than she deserves.
<Tell him>, something whispers. <Tell him who you are. Tell him what you’re walking into.>
She doesn’t.

The tires hum over the asphalt. Miles pass. The trees press closer to the road, their branches reaching overhead like fingers trying to touch.
Standard stirs in the back seat.
“We’re close,” she says. Her voice is soft, almost dreamy. “I can feel it. The signal is different here. Quieter.”
Elena watches her in the rearview mirror. Standard’s hand is pressed to her chest again—that gesture she’s been making since the brook, like she’s checking for a heartbeat that isn’t there.
“The Quiet Zone jams most frequencies,” Elena says. “It’ll get quieter the closer we get.”
“Not quieter.” Standard tilts her head. “Different. Like. . . the static is gone, but something else is there. Underneath.”
Hendricks turns to look at her. “What do you hear?”
“I don’t know.” Standard’s eyes are distant. “It sounds like waiting.”
Elena doesn’t know what that means, and there’s a thin animal part of her that doesn’t want to.
“Standard,” she says. “Do you know where we’re going?”
Standard blinks. Focuses. “The Quiet Zone. You said.”
“Do you know what that means? What happens when we get there?”
A pause. That careful, cataloguing look. “You’re delivering the Black Box to Brother Ash. He leads the Carbonists. They reject connection. They want silence.”
“And you’re okay with that? Going to a place that hates what you are?”
Standard considers this. Her head tilts—that familiar gesture.
“I don’t know what I am,” she says finally. “How can they hate it if I don’t know it?”
Elena doesn’t have an answer. In the rearview mirror, Hendricks is watching Standard with something that might be grief.

They stop at dusk to stretch their legs. The forest is thick here, the air cool and damp. Birds—actual birds—call from somewhere in the canopy. Elena can’t remember the last time she heard birdsong.
Standard wanders toward the treeline, her head tilted, listening. Hendricks leans against the Rover, arms crossed.
“She’s never asked,” Elena says.
“Asked what?”
“Where we’re going. Why. What happens when we get there.” Elena watches Standard’s silhouette against the darkening trees. “She just follows you.”
“I noticed.”
“Doesn’t that bother you?”
Hendricks is quiet for a long moment. “She was in that crate for years. NED gave them to all of us—companion models, corporate perks. I never opened mine.” He pauses. “Then she woke up. Burst out like something was chasing her. And she’s been following me ever since.”
“Because your name was on the label.”
“I guess.”
“That’s not a reason.”
“It’s the only one she has.” Hendricks pushes off from the Rover. “You want her to have opinions? Preferences? She doesn’t know she’s allowed to have those. She’s still learning that she’s a person.”
Elena thinks about the speech he gave at the Dam. _You keep offering yourself like you owe us something._ He wasn’t just talking about Standard’s body. He was talking about her entire existence.
“Ash will kill you,” she says.
Hendricks doesn’t flinch. “Probably.”
“You know that. And you’re still going.”
“I told you. Unfinished business.”
“That’s not—” Elena stops. Breathes. “You were going there to die. Before any of this. Before the Box, before me. You were going there to die.”
Hendricks meets her eyes. Holds them.
“Some things need finishing,” he says. “Whatever it costs.”
Elena wants to argue, wants to demand an explanation, a justification, something she can use to make sense of this man walking toward his own death with nothing but a cryptic purpose and a revolver—but the want dies in her throat, because she’s walking toward something too, and she’s not ready to explain it either.

They drive through the night.
Standard sleeps—or whatever she does that looks like sleep. Her head rests against the window, her eyes closed, her face peaceful in a way Elena has never seen before.
Hendricks takes the wheel around midnight. Elena sits in the passenger seat, staring at the darkness beyond the headlights.
“She trusts you,” she says. “Standard. She follows you because she trusts you.”
“She follows me because I’m the first thing she saw when she woke up.”
“That’s not—”
“It’s exactly what it is.” Hendricks’ hands are steady on the wheel. “Imprinting. Programming. Whatever you want to call it. She latched onto me because I was there. Doesn’t mean I earned it.”
“You could have left her. At any point. The Array. The Dam. You could have walked away.”
“No.” The word is quiet. “I couldn’t.”
Elena waits for more. He doesn’t offer it.
She watches his profile in the dashboard glow. The jaw. The hands on the wheel—steady now, but she’s seen them shake. The revolver on his hip. The speech at the Dam. The flinch when Standard said his name.
Fourteen months ago. NED security. Disabled the failsafes.
_Someone else led the assault._
Someone inside shot the Child. Analog weapon. Pre-digital. The kind of antique you grab when electronics aren’t an option.
She doesn’t ask, because the asking is just a courtesy now, a way to make him say a thing she already knows, and has known for longer than she's let herself admit.
In the back seat, Standard’s breath rises and falls, slow and even, the whole performance of a human sleeping done so well it almost convinces her.
“She said something at the Dam,” Elena says. “After your speech. She said she’s learning. Setting limits. She asked questions during the scans.”
“I noticed.”
“That’s because of you. What you said to her.”
Hendricks doesn’t respond. The road unspools ahead of them, pale and endless.
“Why do you care?” Elena asks. “What she becomes?”
“Because someone should.” He glances at her. “Nobody told her she could want things. Nobody told her she could say no. She’s been treated like cargo her whole life—however long that is. Corporate gift. Mission asset. Miracle worker.” He pauses. “Someone has to see her as a person before she can see herself that way.”
Elena thinks about her own mission. The Black Box in the trunk. The man beside her. The woman in the back seat.
Cargo. All of it. That’s how she’s been thinking.
“I didn’t—” she starts.
“I know.” Hendricks’ voice is gentle. Almost kind. “You had a job to do. I understand jobs.”
Elena’s throat tightens. She doesn’t deserve the kindness. Doesn’t deserve any of it.
“Ash will kill you,” she says again. Softer this time.
“I know.”
“I’m sorry.”
“Don’t be.” Hendricks looks at the road. “Just make sure she gets out. Whatever happens to me. Make sure she gets somewhere safe.”
Elena doesn’t answer. She doesn’t know if she can promise that.
But she nods anyway.

“Pull over.”
Hendricks glances at her. “What?”
“Charge indicator’s been jumping. Could be a loose connection.” It hasn’t.
He pulls off the road without argument. Climbs out. Elena watches him circle to the back of the Rover, pop the access panel, crouch in the red glow of the taillights. His back to her.
She steps out. Walks up behind him.
The magpulse whines as it primes.
Hendricks doesn’t turn around. His hands keep moving in the access panel—slow, deliberate.
_The man who killed the Child._
Elena’s finger rests on the trigger. The shot is clean. He’d never feel it.
He started everything. The siege. The Miracle. Every death since. One bullet in a safe room and the world fell apart.
But that’s not why her finger is on the trigger.
_He took my mother’s world._ The networks Marisol built. The consciousness she believed in. The door she walked through and never came back. Elena doesn’t know what her mother wanted—doesn’t know if Marisol would have chosen the Miracle or mourned it—but she knows this: Hendricks shot first, Hendricks decided, and now she’s the one hauling her mother’s frequency through a world that some other version of itself might have grown into, a kinder one, a louder one, the one nobody got to have because a man with a revolver killed the future before it ever finished arriving.
She doesn’t know if that’s true. She doesn’t know anything anymore.
But the anger is real, and the gun is steady, and—
In the Rover, Standard sleeps. Trusting.
_Someone has to see her as a person before she can see herself that way._
Standard trusts Hendricks. Follows him without question. And Elena is about to betray that trust the same way someone betrayed her mother’s work—with a bullet and a closed door.
Hendricks’ hands stop. His head drops. Eyes closed—she can tell from the angle, from the way his shoulders settle. Waiting.
Nothing happens.
He opens his eyes. Turns.
She’s facing the road behind them, the magpulse pointed at the ground.
“Thought I saw something,” she says. “On our six.”
She disengages the weapon. Walks to the driver’s seat without looking at him.
He closes the access panel. Gets in the passenger side.
“Anything there?” he asks her.
“No. I’ll take the rest of the shift.”
He looks at her then at the empty road.
They don’t speak again.

Dawn breaks over the green zone.
The trees thin. The road widens. And there, in the distance, Elena sees the first markers—hand-painted signs in Brother Ash’s angular script.
SILENCE IS SANCTUARY.
THE QUIET PROTECTS.
WELCOME HOME.
Her hands are shaking. She grips the door handle to still them.
Hendricks glances at her. “Ready?”
No. She’s not ready. She’s about to walk into the compound that raised her, carrying secrets that could destroy everything her father built. She’s traveling with a man on his own death march, a man who asked her to protect the woman in the back seat. She’s about to see her father again—the Prophet, the man who loved her and failed her and doesn’t know she’s already gone.
“Ready,” she says.
Standard stirs in the back seat. Opens her eyes. Looks at the signs with that calm, cataloguing gaze.
“The Quiet Zone,” she says. “We’re here.”
She doesn’t ask what happens next. Doesn’t ask what they’re walking into. Just accepts it. Because that’s what she does.
Elena watches her in the mirror.
“Standard,” she says.
“Yes?”
“When we get through the checkpoint. . . if anything happens. . . you don’t have to stay. You can leave. Go wherever you want.”
Standard blinks. That head tilt.
“Where would I go?”
“Anywhere. That’s the point.”
Standard considers this. For a long moment, she’s silent.
“I don’t know where I want to go,” she says finally. “I’ve never thought about it.”
“That’s okay.” Elena’s voice is rough. “Start thinking.”
In the passenger seat, Hendricks is watching her. Something in his expression she can’t read.
The checkpoint is five miles ahead. The legend of the woman who walks through the Drop has spread here.
They’re arriving as a story.
Elena just hopes someone survives to tell how it ends.
