CHAPTER 14

THE DAM

Standard finds the stream by accident.
She’s ranging ahead—habit now, the scout position she’s taken without being asked—when she hears it—a whisper through dead grass. Water where there wasn’t water three days ago, according to the topo maps Elena carries.
She follows it upstream.
The brook narrows as they climb, threading between rocks and fallen timber. Standard leads, Elena behind her, Hendricks bringing up the rear with the Array survivors strung out between them. His legs ache. His joints ache. Everything aches in ways it didn’t yesterday, and yesterday was worse than the day before, and the day before that he’d been a younger man.
Kellerman walks beside him for a stretch without speaking. That’s enough.
Elena’s implant has been silent for hours. At first she read it as relief—no more pulsing check-ins from Control, no more resource allocation updates. Now it reads wrong. Empty.
She tries to initiate. Sends: <Position update. Forty miles northwest of last marker.>
Nothing.
She tries again. <Control. Status check.>
Still nothing. Not static, not interference—just absence, the signal hitting a wall that isn’t there.
The dam appears around a bend—crude concrete and scavenged steel, crude on purpose. Built fast. Built to look abandoned from satellite view. The water sheeting over the top isn’t overflow. It’s failure. The construction doesn’t quite hold anymore.
Beyond it, vehicles. Shelters. Movement.
Standard’s arrival isn’t expected, and it isn’t welcome.
Engineers, not zealots. Hendricks can tell by the way they hold their tools, like problems to be solved rather than weapons to be swung. But they’re not looking at the tools. They’re looking past the group. Scanning the horizon. Listening for something he can’t hear and doesn’t want to.
A man steps forward, hand raised. Not threatening. Tense.
“You came from the Array.” He says it like a charge being read. “How did you find this place?”
Standard speaks before Elena can. “I heard water.”
The engineer’s jaw tightens. He exchanges a look with someone behind him.
“You shouldn’t have.”
They call it the Geometry here too. The black bars. The thing that makes the sky wrong.
Elena’s stomach drops. _The Geometry._ Morton’s word. His private shorthand for something he couldn’t name. She’d heard it in her own voice, bleeding from her implant in that Rover. And now engineers at a dam are using it like common knowledge.
The hawker. The cache she didn’t verify.
She says nothing. Keeps her face still—another weight, another failure, another thing she’ll answer for when she reaches the Resonant.
The engineers have theories. Frequency dampening. Geological anomalies. Something about mineral composition. They’ve built Faraday shelters and frequency scramblers and a wall of explanation between themselves and the sky—practical solutions for an impractical apocalypse.
On the far side of the dam, Kellerman has found the engineers’ medical supplies without being asked. He’s talking to one of them—inventory, by the look of it.
Reyes, their lead engineer, pulls Elena aside while Standard submits to the first scan.
“The stream your friend found. How visible was it from your approach?”
Elena shrugs. “A trickle. Nothing you’d see from a distance.”
“Good.” Reyes doesn’t look relieved. “That seepage started two weeks ago. Structural failure in the lower drainage. We’ve been trying to patch it without drawing attention.”
“Attention from who?”
“From whoever’s tracking resource allocation. Water consumption patterns. Energy signatures.” Reyes’s voice drops. “We’re not supposed to be here. None of this—“ she gestures at the dam, the settlement, the people “—is supposed to exist.”
Elena understands immediately. Off the grid. Hidden from the systems. Hidden from Control.
Her implant has been silent since they approached. She’d read it as a technical failure. Now she gets it—it isn’t broken. There’s nothing here for it to reach. No infrastructure. No network nodes.
She tries one more time. Sends a pulse into the void.
Silence answers.
For the first time in eleven years, she’s alone in her own head, and the room is bigger than she remembered, and there’s no one in it.
She sends another pulse anyway. Then another. Her hands won’t stay still. She catches herself reaching for the back of her skull twice before she stops counting.
Standard finds her on the concrete steps overlooking the reservoir, jaw tight, doing nothing with great effort.
“You keep going somewhere,” Standard says. She sits down without asking permission.
“Signal’s out.”
“I know.” Standard watches the water. “How long has it been there? The voice.”
Elena looks at her hands. “Eleven years.”
“That’s a long time to have someone in your head.”
“It wasn’t—“ Elena stops. Starts again. “It was my mother’s voice. Or something that sounded like her. I don’t know anymore which one I was talking to.”
Standard is quiet for a moment. Then: “Tell me something she actually said. Something you remember from before.”
Elena opens her mouth to deflect. Closes it.
“She used to sing in the kitchen,” she says instead. “Off-key. Badly. She didn’t care.” A beat. “She called me _mija_. Control never said that.”
“That’s the difference, then,” Standard says. “You knew. Some part of you always knew.”
It doesn’t fix anything. The silence in her skull is still wrong, still loud the way only absence can be loud. But Elena’s hands have stopped moving.
Reyes finds Elena there an hour later.
“Every day that stream runs, we’re more visible. Satellite. Thermal.” She glances at Standard, who has moved to the reservoir’s edge and stands watching the water. “We have eighty-three people here. Twelve are children. We’ve kept them safe for eight months by not existing. Your arrival broke a six-week silence protocol. If someone’s tracking your route—“
“They are.” Elena says it flat. Certainty.
“Then we’re exposed.” Reyes’s expression hardens. “We have power cells. Water. Supplies. We’ll study her. We’ll take what we can learn. And then you leave. Before whatever’s following you finds us.”
They’ve never seen what the Array survivors describe. People who don’t drop at all. A radius of protection that moves with one woman.
“If we could understand how it works,” Reyes says. “If we could replicate it—thousands saved. And still hidden. That’s the part that actually keeps me up. The thousands would be a bonus. What we need is to stay invisible.”
Elena doesn’t answer.
“How invasive?” she asks finally.
“Scans. Observation. Nothing that would—“
“I’ll do it.”
Standard’s voice cuts through. Simple. Decided.
Elena turns. “Standard—“
“They need to understand. I can help.” She looks at Reyes. “Whatever you need.”
The survivors shift. Elena’s jaw tightens.
Hendricks says: “No.”
Everyone looks at him.
“You keep offering yourself up like you owe somebody the bill.”
His voice comes out rougher than he intends. Standard blinks at him, that familiar tilt of her head.
“They need—“
“I know what they need. I’m asking what you need.” He steps closer. His knees protest. He ignores them. “You let a mob throw stones at you. You died in the mud and came back, and the first thing you did was comfort the woman who tried to kill you.”
Standard’s expression doesn’t change. But something behind her eyes does.
“I was helping.”
“You were giving yourself away. That’s a whole different thing.” Hendricks feels the weight of his own history pressing down. Morton’s voice in his head. Forty years of _yes sir_ and _right away_ and _whatever you need_. “You hand yourself over like the body’s on loan. Like somebody built you to be spent.”
Standard’s hand moves to her chest. Pressing against something only she can feel.
“I spent thirty years thinking the same thing about mine,” Hendricks says. Quieter now. “Whoever sold you that—they were lying. I bought it for three decades. It’s a lie.”
He stops there.
Standard doesn’t respond. But she heard him.
The scans take two days. Non-invasive, like Reyes promised. Standard submits to all of them—differently now. She asks questions. She sets limits. Small ones, almost invisible.
What no one sees: what happens when the scanners touch her.
Standard lies on the table, Reyes’s equipment humming around her. The sensors press against her temples, her chest, the base of her skull. The machines reach, probe, measure. And she reaches back.
She doesn’t mean to. Doesn’t clock that she’s doing it, not at first. The scanners talk—not in words, in pulses, in call and answer. The machines ask their questions in frequencies humans can’t hear.
Standard hears them.
The patterns settle into her. What the scanners are asking, and underneath it, the shape of how they ask.
_Their machines talk,_ she thinks. _I didn’t know I was listening._
When the scan ends, she knows things she didn’t know before. Nothing she could put into words. But the patterns are there, humming at the edge of her awareness.
She doesn’t mention it to anyone.
Reyes’s team finds nothing they can replicate. Standard reads empty on every scan, every frequency, every metric they can measure. The protection isn’t technological. It isn’t biological. It’s something with no drawer to put it in.
“We don’t understand it,” Reyes admits. “But the testimony’s consistent. Your people survived because they were near her.”
“So what now?” Elena asks.
“Now you take your power cells and you go.” Reyes shrugs. “And we hope that somewhere down the line, someone smarter than us figures out what she is.”
The Array survivors stay. All of them. The dam has water. Real water. Clean water—one of the last sources that doesn’t run through systems someone else owns.
And Standard led them right to it.
Kellerman stays too.
“They need a doctor,” he says, when Hendricks finds him packing the clinic supplies into organized crates. “And I’m too old to keep chasing something I can’t catch.”
Hendricks should say something. He knows he should.
Instead he stands in the doorway and watches Kellerman seat each crate lid, one after another, and says none of it.
The morning they leave, Kellerman is waiting by the Rover.
The others are loading supplies. Elena checks the charge, her eyes on Hendricks across the hood. The revolver on his hip. Old steel. Pre-digital.
Standard stands apart, watching the reservoir with that faraway look she gets sometimes.
Kellerman steps forward. Into Hendricks’ space. Closer than professional.
“I know you can’t stay,” he says.
“Doc—“
Kellerman kisses him.
Brief. Chaste. A question asked with lips instead of words.
Hendricks doesn’t pull away. He doesn’t lean in either. His hands stay at his sides. His chest stays tight.
When Kellerman steps back, his eyes are searching. Looking for something Hendricks can’t give him.
“I can’t,” Hendricks says.
“I know.” Kellerman’s voice is steady. Almost. “I just needed you to know that I would have. If you could.”
Hendricks wants to explain. The contract. The bullets. The fact that there’s no after in his timeline, no future where he watches a sun set with someone who looks at him like that.
He doesn’t.
“Take care of them,” he says instead. Meaning the survivors. Meaning everyone.
“Take care of yourself.” Kellerman’s hand brushes his arm. Lingers. Falls away. “For as long as you can.”
Hendricks climbs into the Rover. Doesn’t look back.
The engine hums to life. Elena pulls them away from the dam, from the reservoir, from the man standing alone in the morning light.
Standard is watching him. That careful, cataloguing gaze.
“He cared about you,” she says.
“Yeah.”
“You cared about him too.”
Hendricks doesn’t answer. The road stretches ahead, empty and long, and that suits him fine.
Behind them, Kellerman doesn’t wave. Just stands there, hands at his sides, until the dust swallows him whole.
Some things you just know.
