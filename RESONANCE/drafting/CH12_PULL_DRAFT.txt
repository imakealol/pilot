CHAPTER 12

THE PULL

The fuel dies two miles out.

Not the long decline of the day before — no gauge ticking down, no warning light. The engine just stops. The Rover rolls a few feet on momentum and settles onto broken asphalt.

The Array is still visible behind them. A smear of solar panels on the horizon, catching the early light.

Elena tries the ignition. Nothing. Tries again.

"That's it," Kellerman says from the back. "Hydrogen burns faster than the fusion core. Dirty fuel."

Elena's hand stays on the key. Then she takes it out.

Outside, the wasteland is flat and pale in the morning cool, the black bars overhead pulling at the light. In every direction: nothing. No landmarks. No features.

Standard opens her door.

---

Hendricks takes the wheel. He can steer but not push — his legs won't hold him in the open air for long and everyone knows it without saying so. He rolls the window down.

Elena takes the left side. Standard takes the right, wrapping the tow hook around both hands. Kellerman falls in beside Elena. The survivors — those who can stand — spread along the back and sides, palms flat against the hull.

They push.

The morning is cool enough that it feels survivable.

---

The boy is first.

He doesn't fall. He just slows, and his hands slide off the hull, and he stands there in the road with his arms hanging. His eyes are open. He looks like a man listening for something he can't hear.

Kellerman is beside him in three steps. Gets his arm over his shoulder. Opens the cargo door and puts him in the shade.

When Kellerman comes back, he doesn't say anything. He puts his hands back on the Rover.

They keep pushing.

---

The sun comes up properly around the third hour.

The black bars drink the light but not the heat. The heat comes anyway, radiating off the asphalt, rising from the pale dirt on either side of the road. The wasteland holds it like a bowl.

Nessa goes next. Then one of the other survivors — Standard doesn't know his name. He makes it another half mile before Kellerman puts him in.

Four pushing now. Then three.

They push.

---

Elena doesn't say she's stopping. She just isn't pushing anymore.

Standard feels it in the weight — a subtle increase, the load redistributing. She looks left. Elena is walking beside the Rover with her hands at her sides, her face down.

"Get in," Standard says.

"I'm fine."

"You're not pushing."

Elena looks at her hands like she's surprised to find them empty. Then she gets in without arguing.

---

The last survivor stops somewhere after that. Standard becomes aware of it only as weight — a little more, redistributed again.

Two.

Kellerman holds until midafternoon.

He doesn't decide to stop. Standard sees him slow, sees his hands drift lower on the hull, sees the tremor start in his arms. He keeps going after the tremor starts. He keeps going after it gets worse.

Then he stops. Both hands braced on the Rover's side, head down, breathing.

"Keep east," he says. "If there's anything out here it'll be east. Lower ground."

Standard nods.

He opens the door and gets in.

---

Red text begins scrolling at the edge of her vision — warnings in a language she doesn't know how to read. She blinks them away. They come back. She blinks them away again.

Her right knee catches. Locks. Releases. She adjusts her stride.

The hum inside her is quieter than it was this morning.

It has always been there — a low, constant thing behind her sternum. Background. The sound of something running. She doesn't know what it means. She only knows it's getting quieter, the way a voice gets quieter when someone walks away.

The warnings fill more of her vision. She can't blink them away anymore. She moves through them.

Her left leg stutters. She locks it straight and drags it and it comes back online and she keeps moving.

The hum is almost gone.

She doesn't think about this. There's nothing to think. The weight is behind her and the road is ahead of her and those are the only facts that have any surface to hold.

She pulls.

---

Something changes underfoot.

She doesn't process it — her vision has narrowed to the chain and the three feet of road directly ahead. But her feet feel it. The asphalt is different. Less give, then a different give. Not the packed dryness she's been moving on for hours.

She keeps pulling.

The scrub at the roadside changes. She doesn't notice this either, exactly. Just different dead things than before. Things that dried slower.

The ground darkens.

A shade. Then another. The road surface changes color under her feet — pale gray to something with more brown in it, then darker, then darker again.

Her feet leave prints.

---

The ground goes soft.

Her right foot sinks an inch. Her left. She keeps moving.

Darker. Wetter. The ground pulling at her feet now.

She stops.

Something ahead. The ground becomes something else — a surface that reflects. Low and still, the color of old pewter.

She goes down.

Not a fall. A cessation. Her knees find mud. Her hands, still curled around the shape of the chain, press into the soft ground. Her cheek presses into it too.

The hum goes silent.

The water is at her fingertips.

She doesn't know.
