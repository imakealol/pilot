THE PROBLEM WITH THE ORIGINAL CHAPTER 5

The original chapter attempts to do too much in too little space: Elena's 
guilt over her dead crew, the checkpoint problem, Standard's introduction 
as a Void, a Control beat, and the Geometry's arrival all compete for the 
same real estate. The larger structural problem is motivational: Elena, 
Hendricks, and Standard each independently have ways around the checkpoint, 
making their convergence feel engineered rather than inevitable. Elena has 
official armor and a Rover already waved through the outer perimeter. 
Hendricks (in the original) has a secret service corridor. Standard drifts 
in. None of them have a genuine reason to be stuck at the same chokepoint.

The fix splits the original chapter into two and rebuilds the logic from 
the ground up.

CHAPTER 5 REWRITE

TITLE: THE QUEUE (retain)

CORE LOGIC:
Elena is in the Priority lane, engine off, pre-flag — working a 
potential problem with Control before it becomes an actual one. Her hot 
implant will trigger the checkpoint scanner. She needs a solution before 
she pulls forward.

Control is proactive here, not reactive. Its priority is getting the Black 
Box to Detroit — that is Ash's agenda and Control serves it. The checkpoint 
flag is a threat to that delivery. Control suggests Elena run an active 
scan of the queue for useful resources.

Elena scans. She finds a Void — a hole in the data, an absence where there 
should be signal. It's traveling with an old man. Both are clearly trying 
to hitch a ride with anyone who'll take them.

Control identifies the tactical application immediately: Standard's 
absence-of-signal can swallow Elena's hot implant at the checkpoint. Walk 
her through alongside the Rover and the scanner reads nothing anomalous.

Elena goes to acquire them. This is purely tactical. Cold. She doesn't 
know who the old man is — he's just the man who comes with the Void.

Standard's Void hits Elena physically when she gets close — the implant 
goes silent for the first time since NED. Not peace. Absence. Like going 
deaf.

Elena pulls them both into the Rover. Hendricks and Standard board.

The moment Standard enters the Rover, Control goes silent. Elena notices. 
No explanation available. She is on her own.

Chapter ends here — on that silence, the hum of the Box, two strangers 
she doesn't understand, and no voice in her ear.

BEAT LIST:
1. Elena in Priority lane, engine off, pre-flag. Working the problem with 
   Control.
2. Control proactively suggests scanning the masses for useful resources.
3. Elena runs the scan. Finds the Void — a hole in the data traveling with 
   an old man. Both trying to hitch a ride.
4. Control identifies the application: Void swallows her hot implant signal 
   at the checkpoint.
5. Elena approaches. Purely tactical.
6. Standard's Void hits her physically up close. Implant goes silent.
7. She pulls them both. Hendricks is just the man who comes with her.
8. They board. Control goes silent the moment Standard enters.
9. Chapter ends on Elena alone with that silence.

KEY CONSTRAINTS:
- Elena does NOT know who Hendricks is. That reveal happens at the outpost.
- No service corridor. That plot mechanism is removed entirely.
- Control goes silent the moment Standard boards and does not return 
  while Standard is in proximity.
- Control's motivation is the Black Box reaching Detroit — serving Ash. 
  It does not know Standard is Marisol. It does not know what Standard is.
  It is using her as a tactical tool without understanding what it's doing.
- Standard and Hendricks are there because they're trying to hitch a ride. 
  Simple. No further justification needed.
- Retain the three empty seats beat (Goff, Otis, Aikin) — Elena's guilt 
  is still the emotional undercurrent of the chapter.
- Retain the Black Box hum / 287.3 Hz establishing beat.
- Retain the physical description of the queue and the Depo environment.

CHAPTER 6 REWRITE

TITLE: TO BE DETERMINED (original title may need revisiting)

CORE LOGIC:
The plan fails at the checkpoint. The Void reads as anomalous in its own 
right — absence is as weird as presence. They're asked to pull over.

Then the Geometry arrives.

This is their first appearance in the novel. They are not violent. They 
are running an audit — passing judgment, scanning at a scale and frequency 
that is simply overwhelming to biological and augmented nervous systems. 
No one dies. Everyone in range is pummeled by the power of the scan and 
passes out. Guards, refugees, everyone.

Except Standard.

Standard is unaffected. She assesses. She gets in the driver's seat and 
drives them through the unmanned checkpoint toward the outpost while her 
two passengers are unconscious.

Elena wakes up moving, having cleared the checkpoint, with no idea how.

BEAT LIST:
1. Approaching checkpoint. Elena running the plan mentally — no Control 
   to verify anything.
2. Checkpoint scan. The Void flags as anomalous. Asked to pull over.
3. Plan has failed. Tense wait in the Rover.
4. The Geometry arrives. Audit begins. The frequency hits.
5. Elena, Hendricks, guards, refugees — all down. Overwhelmed, not dead.
6. Standard unaffected. Assesses. Gets in the driver's seat.
7. Drives through the unmanned checkpoint toward the outpost.
8. Elena wakes up moving.

KEY CONSTRAINTS:
- The Geometry is not introduced as an antagonist. They are indifferent, 
  vast, running an audit. The word "corrected" from the original chapter 
  (the frequency "trying to rearrange their insides as if they're wrong / 
  as if they are being corrected") is exactly right — retain that framing.
- No mass death. The drop is overwhelming, not lethal.
- Standard driving is her first act of agency in the novel. It is quiet, 
  deliberate, and unexplained. She does not narrate it. She just does it.
- Elena waking up to find them already past the checkpoint — with no idea 
  how — is the chapter's final beat and its emotional payload.
- The deletion rectangles in the sky from the original chapter should be 
  retained as the Geometry's visual signature.

RETROACTIVE SIGNIFICANCE (for tonal awareness, not to be stated in prose)

Control unknowingly engineers the reunion of a mother and daughter while 
serving the man who will eventually try to destroy them both. Standard's 
Void silences the thing that wore Marisol's voice for eleven years — every 
time it goes quiet near her, that is happening. The reader won't know this 
on first read. On reread it is devastating. The prose should earn that 
weight without announcing it.
