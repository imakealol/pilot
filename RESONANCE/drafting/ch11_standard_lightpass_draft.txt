CHAPTER 11

THE WITNESS

Standard doesn't sleep. She sits in the clinic through the night, watching Hendricks breathe.
The fever comes and goes—she can see it in the color of his face, the flush and the pale and the flush again. His breathing is wet. When he rolls onto his side, the bandage pulls tight across the node wound and he makes a sound she doesn't think he's aware of. She doesn't touch it. Kellerman left instructions, and she follows them: water when he can take it, the fever medication at intervals, nothing else. She sits on the stool beside the cot and watches and waits.
Kellerman crosses the compound twice in the night. She tracks him through the clinic window—careful, unhurried movement between containers, a pause before each door. He doesn't come in. Whatever he's checking, Hendricks isn't it.
The sky begins to gray. Elena appears in the doorway with her magpulse in hand.
"Fuel line's cut. Overnight." Her jaw is tight. "Two hours. Maybe three if we crawl."
"Then we go now." Standard is standing.
Kellerman comes through the door behind Elena—he's been awake, the clinic walls are thin—and moves directly to Hendricks' cot.
"He needs another day—"
"We don't have another day." Elena has Hendricks' arm over her shoulder. He sways. Catches himself. "If we stay until he's ready, we won't be able to leave."
They're halfway to the door when the crowd fills the courtyard.
Twenty of them. The big man from dinner at the front with his wrench. Vera with her scanner. Nessa with the pipe.
Elena's magpulse is in the Rover. Ten meters away.
Standard steps forward before Elena can position herself. She puts herself where they're looking.
"You're not leaving," the big man says.
"We paid for passage," Elena says.
"You paid for fuel. You didn't pay for answers." His voice climbs. "When the bars came, we dropped. All of us. The whole town in the dirt with our teeth bit through. But her? Yeah, we heard of her. Word came a woman who stood. And she stood there. She didn't drop. So tell me what that makes her."
"Let them scan me," Standard says.
Elena spins. "No."
"They won't let us go until they see." Standard walks to Vera before Elena can stop her and holds out her arms.
Vera's hands shake. The scanner moves across Standard's arms, her chest, her head. Data scrolls across the cracked screen.
"She's human." Vera looks up. "Biologically human—heart, lungs, brain activity, all normal. But no implants. No signal. No network echo. Everyone has something, even if they've been scrubbed. A trace, a ghost." She swallows. "She's empty. Like she was never connected to anything."
"That's not human," the big man says.
Kellerman pushes through the crowd. He moves between Standard and the mob—puts his body there, turns to face them.
"Stop. This isn't medicine. This is a witch trial. You want to cut her open because she survived something you didn't. That's not justice. That's grief looking for a throat to squeeze."
The crowd wavers. The big man's grip on the wrench loosens.
Nessa throws the first stone.
It catches Standard on the shoulder. The second hits Kellerman in the back. The third shatters on the Rover's hull. The crowd surges.
The sky begins to hum.
She's felt this before—at the checkpoint, before the Drop. The frequency rising before the fall.
"No—" Elena, behind her. "No, no, no—"
The hum rises. The black bars pulse once—a ripple of deeper darkness moving through them like blood through a vein. Then the frequency hits.
The crowd drops. All of them, mid-swing, mid-scream. The big man folds three feet from Standard. Vera collapses over her scanner. Nessa goes down with the next stone still in her hand. Elena drops. Hendricks drops.
Kellerman staggers—one hand finds Standard's sleeve, his knees bend, his face goes the color of chalk. He doesn't drop.
Standard moves.
The black bars overhead are lower.
Not falling. Lower. They hung at altitude all night and now they don't.
She sweeps the courtyard. Elena near the Rover. Hendricks three feet from the clinic door. The survivors scattered between—the boy closest, Nessa further out. The big man twitching.
At the far end of the fence—chain-link, panels, posts—the metal folds. Along an axis the world doesn't have. The fence doesn't collapse. It edits. East to west, clean as a zipper, the structure going from existing to never-having-been-built.
Kellerman has stopped moving. His mouth is open. No sound.
"Move," Standard says.
He moves.
She gets Elena first. Under the arms, dragged to the passenger seat. _Count them. Elena. Hendricks. The boy._ Back for Hendricks—she gets him into the cargo bed. _Three._ The boy next, Kellerman hauling him by the armpits with legs that barely work. Standard takes the boy's weight without breaking stride and loads him in.
The first container folds. Walls drawn inward to a line, the line to a point, the point to nothing. The clinic where she sat all night. Gone before she can name what she's seeing.
They work without speaking. She moves. He does what he can.
The big man is stirring. The frequency fades faster than it arrived.
Three more containers fold in the same breath. A row of solar panels lifts, rotates, and is not.
Hendricks, the boy, two more within reach. She goes back—Nessa, still down, the unthrown stone lying open beside her hand—gets her under the arms and drags, and the next one is the man she's never spoken to, face-down where the courtyard runs out toward the fold, and her hands are already on him before she has finished thinking the distance, the time, the count that is supposed to mean she can reach him and—
She pulls him in beside the others.
Two figures fold along the same axis as the panels—shoulders to a line, line to a point, point to nothing. Where they were is courtyard.
The fold is twenty meters off. Closing.
Six. That's what the radius gave her.
_No further. Six and the door. Don't go back past the door._
"The fuel." She turns.
Kellerman is already at the undercarriage—found the panel, or guessed it. She hears the valve release, the hiss of the reservoir connecting. The fuel gauge, dead since dawn, flickers and climbs and stops at eighteen percent.
"That's everything." He steps back.
Behind them, the big man is on his hands and knees. The fold is closer to him than to the Rover. _He's outside the six. He was always outside the six._
"Get in." Standard opens the passenger door.
He gets in. She's in the driver's seat before he pulls the door shut. The gate comes up fast and then it's behind them. Behind it, the courtyard folds.
Elena comes around somewhere past the third mile. She sits up slowly, takes in the cargo bed—Hendricks, the survivors, Kellerman facing rearward with two fingers on someone's wrist.
"How many?"
"Six. Plus Hendricks."
"How many dropped?"
"Everyone else."
Elena looks at the fuel gauge. Fifteen percent and falling. She doesn't close her eyes.
"Back in the common area," she says. "When the old woman said you were at the queue—half that room stopped moving."
Standard keeps her eyes on the road.
"They heard the same thing the big man heard and they stepped back instead of forward." Elena turns to face the windshield. "That's going to happen everywhere we go. People are going to hear that you survived what killed everyone else and they're going to decide it means something. Some of them will want you dead for it. Some of them will want to touch the hem of your coat." A beat. "That second group is useful. People make way for things they think are sacred."
Standard is quiet for a moment. "It gets people killed."
"Kellerman made his own choice."
"The ones who stepped back. They would have put themselves between me and the crowd eventually." Standard's hands don't move on the wheel. "Not because of me. Because of what they needed me to be."
Elena doesn't answer right away.
"That's how it works," she says finally.
Standard doesn't respond.
The road ahead is empty. The black bars hang still.
Standard drives.
