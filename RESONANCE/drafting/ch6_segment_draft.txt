
CHAPTER 6

THE REDACTED SKY

The world, it seemed, was being edited.
Not destroyed.
Revised.
Standard sits in the silence that follows the scream. It is not a quiet silence. It is a vacuum silence—the sudden, violent depressurization of a space that was previously full of noise. The hum of the queue is gone. The weeping of the refugees is gone. Even the rain seems to have lost its rhythm, falling in a dumb, static drizzle that hits the windshield without sound.
She looks out the window.
The sky is gone.
Where the gray clouds of the Deposit had been, there are now perfect, absolute rectangles of black. They do not look like colonizing ships. They look like redaction bars drawn across a document by a heavy hand. They hang in the atmosphere, motionless, absorbing the light of the neon signs below.
Standard checks her hands. The blood on her cuticles has dried to a dark, flaking crust. Pulse. Steady. Breathing. Even.
She looks at the woman called Elena, slumped face down on the ground. Her breathing is shallow, hitching every few seconds as if her body is trying to remember how to pull air.
Standard gets out.
In the Rover, Hendricks is not moving at all. She reaches in the open window on his side. She places two fingers on his neck, right over the pulse point, ignoring the bloody towel. His skin is cold. The beat underneath is there, but it is thready. A wire pulled too tight, vibrating in the wind.
He is broken. They are all broken.
The realization settles on her with the weight of a physical object:
Even the end of the world didn't want me.
A vibration rattles her teeth. Not the deep, bone-shaking thunder of the storm, but something higher. Sharper.
She looks toward the cargo bed.
The Black Box. The heavy mass Elena called merchandise. It is humming.
Standard approaches the rear hatch. She shouldn’t touch it. The woman, Elena, treated it like a bomb. But the silence in the Rover is unbearable, and the hum is the only thing that feels alive.
Standard places her ruined hand on the matte-gray metal.
The vibration stops.
It doesn’t fade. It cuts out. As if the box is holding its breath.
Standard waits. There is a pressure behind her eyes—not pain, exactly, but a sense of density. As if the air inside the car has doubled on itself, packed tight, pressing out against the glass and against the backs of her eyes both at once.
She pulls her hand back. The hum returns, tentative, quiet.
Outside, something moves.
It descends from the redacted sky not like a bird, but like a cursor. A shape. A perfect tetrahedron of black stone, rotating slowly, point down. It is the size of a house. It makes no sound. It displaces no air.
It floats over the queue of unconscious bodies. It pauses over a cluster of guards. A beam of pale, violet light sweeps over them—clinical, fast. Scanning stock.
Then it drifts toward the Rover.
Standard looks at Elena in the mud. She picks her up—dead weight, rain-soaked—and folds her back into the driver's seat. Then she opens the rear door and gets in.
Standard does not move. She does not breathe. She sits perfectly still, her hands in her lap, and watches the geometry approach.
The violet light hits the hood of the Rover. It washes over the engine block, analyzing the fusion cells, the shielding, the heat.
Then it hits the glass.
Then it hits her.
It feels like fingers inside her skull.
Standard gasps, her back arching off the seat. It is a violation so total, so intimate, she wants to tear her own skin off to escape it. It isn’t pain. It is access. Something is rummaging through her, pulling at her one piece at a time, and everything it reaches comes out empty, comes out wrong, comes out—
She doesn’t hear words. She feels them as nausea—something heavy settling at the base of her skull, pressing, wrong. It feels like swallowing a stone.
The violet light snaps off.
The tetrahedron rotates. It dismisses her. It floats away, moving down the line toward the next cluster of bodies.
Standard slumps back against the seat, shivering.
She really is trash. She is furniture left behind in a condemned building.
She looks up.
In the rearview mirror, eyes are watching her.
Elena.
The woman hasn’t moved. Her head is still resting on the steering wheel. Her body is limp. But her eyes are open. They are bloodshot, wide, and locked on Standard’s face.
She saw.
She saw the geometry hover. She saw the light. She saw Standard survive the inspection that leveled an army.
*No te me vayas*, she says toward the rearview. Barely a sound. Hendricks can’t hear her.
Standard stares back. She doesn’t know what to say. So she says nothing.
Through the side window, movement catches her periphery. Standard turns.
In the mud, ten yards away, a figure is lying on its side. A refugee. Wrapped in layers of plastic and wool. The person—a child, maybe, or a small woman—is frozen, limbs locked in the seizure of the Drop.
But their eyes are open.
They are looking at the Rover. Looking at Standard.
The refugee stares at the woman sitting upright in the glass box while the angels of the apocalypse float by. They stare with a terrifying, blank awe.
Standard wants to open the window and shout it across the mud. *No. I am not special. I am just omitted.*
But she can’t.
A gasp tears through the Rover.
Elena lurches upright, inhaling like she’s breaking surface water. She flails, her hands scrabbling at the dashboard, her legs kicking out.
“Contact!” she screams. “Contact, front!”
She grabs for her weapon.
“There is no contact,” Standard says. Her voice sounds rusty. “It’s gone.”
Elena freezes. She looks around wildly—at the black rectangles in the sky, at the bodies in the mud, at the silent, vibrating Rover. She clutches her chest, her fingers digging into the armor over her sternum.
“The frequency,” Elena whispers. “It stopped.”
“It finished,” Standard corrects.
Elena turns in her seat. She looks at Standard. The fear in her eyes is raw, undisguised. She looks at Standard like she’s looking at a monster.
“Why are you awake?”
“I don’t know.”
“You. . . the drone. I saw it. It spoke to you.”
“It scanned me,” Standard says. The words come out flat, sorted, in the order they happened. “It determined I was not worth removing.”
Elena swallows. She wipes a line of blood from her nose with the back of her hand. The soldier mask is trying to reassemble itself, piece by cracked piece. She looks at the Black Box. She checks the readout on the dashboard.
“Fusion cells are at forty percent,” she mutters. “Shielding held. Barely.”
She looks at Hendricks.
The old man is slumped against the door, his head at a wrong angle. He looks like a pile of laundry.
Elena’s expression hardens. It’s the look she wore in the tower when she left the bodies of her team behind. The math of survival.
“Check him,” Elena says.
“He’s alive,” Standard says. “His pulse is weak.”
“Can he wake up?”
“I don’t know.”
Elena nods. This is her chance, and she takes the inventory of it cold. She couldn’t be blamed for letting him on in the first place—that was before she knew he was who he is. Better like this than on his knees on the side of the road, because that was the only other version, the one where she does it herself. The man who killed her whole team.
She reaches for the door handle. “Help me get him out.”
Standard blinks. “Get him out?”
"We're heavy. The cells drain faster with weight. We have fifty miles to the next outpost and the terrain is going to be hell." Elena's voice is shaking, but the words are precise. "He's dying. He's slowing us down. If we dump the weight, we make it."
She opens her door. The wet, cold air floods the cabin.
“Come on,” Elena says. “Grab his legs.”
Standard does not move. She looks at Hendricks. She looks at the gray skin, the bloody towel, the hands that shook so badly he couldn’t hold a glass. She remembers the way he looked at her in the apartment. The way he came back down the stairs.
She doesn’t feel sadness. She feels. . . structural. As if a load-bearing wall has just been named, and naming it is the same as deciding whether the building stands.
Standard unbuckles her belt, then Hendricks. She opens the door and starts to help the barely conscious man out.
Seeing this, Elena reaches her arm back and places it on Standard’s shoulder.
“Where the hell are you going?”
“Doing what you want,” Standard says.
“Excuse me?”
“If he stays, I stay.”
“He is dead weight!” Elena screams, the panic finally cracking the soldier’s voice. “Look at him! He’s a corpse we haven’t buried yet! I am not dying out here for a stranger!”
“He is not a stranger,” Standard says. “He is. . . my father.”
Elena does a double take. That couldn’t be true. Could it? The word sits there in the cabin, too big, too clean, the kind of claim a person doesn’t make unless it costs them nothing or everything.
“He’s unconscious!”
“Yes, it appears that way. We are sorry to have inconvenienced you.”
Elena stares at her. Her chest is heaving. Her hand drops to her hip. This woman—this Void—isn’t going to make it easy. There is no version where she leaves whatever-the-hell-she-is here in the mud. The options are dwindling, and Elena has spent her whole life watching options dwindle, and they always dwindle toward the same place: a weapon.
Elena draws the magpulse. It’s a heavy, ugly weapon. She points it at Standard’s chest. The barrel is shaking. The whine of the charging capacitor fills the small space.
“I am the mission commander,” Elena says. Her teeth are bared. “I am telling you to push him out and get the hell back in the Rover. Or I leave you both here.”
Standard looks at the gun. The distance. The angle. Elena’s hands.
She’s bluffing.
“You can’t drive,” Standard says.
“What?”
“Your hands. The tremor. It's in your eyes too—your pupils aren't tracking together.” Standard speaks calmly, plainly. “You will crash within two miles. You need a driver.”
“I can drive,” Elena snaps.
“No. You can’t.” Standard leans back. “I can drive. I can operate this vehicle. So here is the deal,” Standard says. “If he stays, I stay. And you die two miles down the road.”
Elena’s finger tightens on the trigger. The hum of the weapon rises.
“Don’t test me, Standard,” Elena whispers. “You don’t know the day I’m having.”
Standard waits, standing by the Rover. She does not blink. She does not know whose seats they are sitting in, or what it cost to put them there. She knows the gun, the angle, the woman’s hands.
From the back seat, a sound. A cough. Wet and deep.
“That’s not. . . the stick. . . you think it is. . . princess. You’re better off. . . using a carrot. That one’s got a death wish. Trust.”
Elena flinches. She spins toward the back. Then looks back at Standard who shrugs.
“We’re kinda’ having a day ourselves.”
Hendricks is not sitting up. His eyes are barely slits. His head is still lolling against the window. He looks like he’s speaking from the bottom of a well.
“Saw you. . .” He wheezes. “At the gate. You pull the piston. . . toward you. . . when you check it.”
Elena stares at him. The gun wavers.
“You don’t. . . shoot people,” Hendricks whispers. “You just. . . carry boxes.”
Silence. Whatever he knows about the people who carry boxes, he keeps it behind his teeth.
Elena looks at the gun in her hand, at the old man, who can barely breathe, but who just dismantled her with a sentence.
For a moment, nobody moves. Elena looks at Hendricks. Hendricks looks at Elena. The haunting low howl of the magpulse sustains eternally.
She lowers the weapon, depowers and holsters. Standard takes the hint and secures Hendricks before jogging to the driver’s side.
Elena climbs into the passenger seat and punches open the compartment to pull out the med kit. Her hands are shaking badly enough that the latch defeats her on the first try. Standard leans across and opens it.
“I’ve got it,” Elena says.
Standard already has the index, tilting it toward the window. STIM-8 for shock. CHELATION for radiation. ANTICONV for seizures. COAG-FAST for bleeding.
“It’s color-coded,” Elena says. “Idiot-proof.”
Standard turns the kit.
*Nothing for alien frequency melted my freaking illegal implant.*
“Blue,” Standard says. “The dampener. Neural overload.”
Elena looks back at her, narrowing.
Standard reads the look and can’t place it. She gave a correct answer. The answer is on the label. The label is in her hand. She turns the question over and finds nothing under it, no reason for the face Elena is making.
She looks back at the kit. One for Elena. She considers whether she needs one too. Doesn’t think so. Or maybe Hendricks—
“There’s only one,” Elena blurts and stabs her thigh through her fatigues. She pulls her knees to her chest to make herself as small as she can. “Drive.”
Standard goes over the console. It is intuitive, and she fits herself into the seat. Her hands find the wheel. Her foot finds the pedal.
The machine responds. It feels natural. Like a thing her body remembers and she doesn't.
She puts the Rover in gear. The headlights cut through the rain, illuminating the field of bodies. Standard does not look at them. She keeps her eyes on the road, or what’s left of it.
Elena’s head drops back against the headrest. "What happened?"
Standard gives her the sequence. The geometry descending. The light. The scan. Just the facts, in order.
"And you?" Elena asks.
Standard doesn’t answer. She keeps her eyes on the road.
"Which direction?"
"East," Elena says. "Stay east."
Elena's eyes roll back and she’s crashing. “Do you. . . even know. . . where. . .” Elena slurs. And then she’s out.
There’s only one road for days.
Should be able to figure it out.
She presses the accelerator, and they move.
