CHAPTER 11

THE WITNESS

Standard doesn't sleep.

She sits in the Rover through the night, watching the compound through the windshield. The black bars overhead drink the starlight. Kellerman crossed between containers twice — once at midnight, once near dawn. He paused at the clinic door both times and glanced toward the Rover.

She watched him. That's all.

The sky begins to gray.

---

Elena emerges from the common area looking like she hasn't slept either. She crosses directly to the Rover, slides in.

"Fuel line's cut."

"When?"

"Night." Elena's jaw is tight. "We have two hours. Three if we crawl."

Standard is already opening her door. "Then we go now."

---

The clinic smells sharper in the morning. Some of the cots have been cleared. Standard doesn't look at the sheets.

Hendricks is sitting up. Kellerman beside him, fingers on his wrist.

"—should rest another day—"

"We can't." Elena is already at the cot. "Someone cut our fuel line. We have two hours."

Kellerman's eyes go to the other cots. The shapes still under blankets.

"He's not ready—"

"Neither are we." Elena gets Hendricks' arm over her shoulder. He sways. Catches himself.

They're halfway to the door when the crowd fills the courtyard.

---

Twenty of them. The big man at the front with his wrench. Vera with her scanner. Nessa, the woman with the pipe.

Elena's magpulse is in the Rover. Ten meters away.

Standard steps forward before Elena can position herself. Let them look at her.

"You're not leaving," the big man says.

"We paid for passage," Elena says.

"You paid for fuel. You didn't pay for answers." His voice cracks before it reaches anger. Not rage — grief. "My wife dropped. My brother dropped. Everyone I ever loved dropped. And she just stood there. Watching."

"Let them scan me," Standard says.

Elena spins. "No."

"They won't let us go until they see."

Standard walks to Vera. Holds out her arms.

---

Vera's hands shake.

The scanner moves over Standard's arms, her chest, her head. Data spits onto the cracked screen.

"She's human." Vera looks up. "Biologically human. Heart, lungs, brain activity — all normal. But there's no implants. No signal. No network echo. Everyone has something, even if they've been scrubbed. A trace. A ghost." She swallows. "She's empty. Like she was never connected to anything."

"That's not human," the big man whispers.

Kellerman pushes through the crowd.

"Stop." He moves between Standard and the mob. "This isn't medicine. This is a witch trial. You want to cut her open because she survived something you didn't. That's not justice. That's grief looking for a throat to squeeze."

The crowd wavers. The big man's grip on the wrench loosens.

Nessa throws the first stone.

---

It catches Standard on the shoulder. The second hits Kellerman in the back. The third shatters on the Rover's hull.

The crowd surges.

The sky begins to hum.

---

She's felt this before. At the checkpoint. Before the Drop.

"No—" Elena, behind her. "No, no, no—"

The hum rises. The black bars pulse once — a ripple of deeper darkness passing through them like blood through a vein.

Then the frequency hits.

The crowd drops.

All of them. Mid-swing, mid-scream. The big man folds three feet from Standard. Vera collapses over her scanner. Nessa goes down with the next stone still in her hand.

Elena drops.

Hendricks drops.

Kellerman staggers — one hand finding Standard's sleeve, knees bent, face the color of chalk. He doesn't drop.

Standard is already moving.

---

She sweeps the courtyard. Elena down near the Rover. Hendricks three feet from the clinic door. The survivors scattered between — the boy closest, Nessa further out, the others beyond her. The big man is already twitching.

Elena first. She's seizing, closest to the Rover. Standard gets her under the arms and drags her to the passenger seat. Goes back.

Kellerman has a boy by the armpits, hauling him across the dirt with legs that barely work. Standard takes the boy's weight without breaking stride and carries him to the cargo bed.

They work without speaking. She moves. He does what he can.

The big man is already stirring. The frequency fades faster than it arrived.

Four in the Rover. Hendricks in the back. Elena in the seat. Two more within reach.

She goes back. Nessa — still down, the unthrown stone lying open beside her hand. Standard gets her under the arms. Drags. Gets the last one, a man she's never spoken to, and pulls him in beside the others.

Six. That's what the radius gave her.

"The fuel." She turns.

Kellerman is already at the undercarriage — he's found the panel, or guessed it. She hears the valve release, the hiss of the reservoir connecting. The fuel gauge, dead since dawn, flickers. Climbs. Stops at eighteen percent.

"That's everything." He steps back.

Behind them, the big man is on his hands and knees.

Standard opens the passenger door. "Get in."

He gets in. She's in the driver's seat before he's pulled the door shut. The gate comes up fast and then it's behind them.

---

Elena comes around somewhere past the third mile. She sits up. Takes in the cargo bed — Hendricks, the survivors, Kellerman in the back facing rearward, two fingers on someone's wrist.

"How many?"

"Six. Plus Hendricks."

"How many dropped?"

"Everyone else."

Elena looks at the fuel gauge. Fifteen percent and falling.

She closes her eyes. Opens them. Doesn't say anything else.

The road ahead is empty. The black bars hang still.

Standard drives.
