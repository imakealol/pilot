
CHAPTER 38

THE BEACONS

The deletion zones open like wounds across the solar system.

From New Geneva, Dante watches the calculations resolve—trajectories plotted backwards through probability foam, exit points mapped to intercept ships that left Earth years ago. The math is beautiful in the way only impossible things can be. Each beacon a message in a bottle thrown not into the sea but into time itself.

"Launching," he says.

The beacons fall into the folds.

[TIMESTAMP: 2392.08.14 - 03:17:22]
[LOCATION: DEEP SPACE - EN ROUTE KEPLER-442B]
[VESSEL: NED-MERIDIAN-7 "SUNWARD"]

The beacon emerges three hundred meters off the port bow.

Yuki Tanaka is mid-shift when the proximity alarm screams. She's been awake for six weeks subjective—the last leg of a journey that started when her daughter was three. Her daughter is ten now. Will be ten when Yuki arrives. If she arrives.

The object tumbles in the void, catching starlight. Not debris. Too precise. Too intentional.

"What the hell is that?"

Her navigator—a NED unit she calls Eighteen—runs scans. "It's... broadcasting. Some kind of frequency. 287.3 Hz."

The message fills her bridge. A voice she doesn't recognize, speaking words that don't make sense. Deletion zones. Probability foam. A door that needs to open. And then another voice—female, steady, raw with something that sounds like defiance:

*I know who holds my leash. It's the people I love.*

Then the first voice again:

*We need volunteers.*

Tanaka looks at her hands. At Eighteen's display. One human, one AI—standard crew for a cargo run that was supposed to be routine.

"How long do we have to decide?"

"The window closes in four minutes."

She thinks of her daughter. Ten years old. Waiting.

"Plot the intercept course," she says. "Let's see what we're dying for."

[TIMESTAMP: 2394.11.02 - 18:44:09]
[LOCATION: DEEP SPACE - EN ROUTE TRAPPIST-1E]
[VESSEL: NED-MERIDIAN-12 "OUTLAW PLANET"]

The beacon emerges inside the cockpit.

There's no time to react. The fold opens wrong—a fraction of a degree, a miscalculation across light-years—and the transmitter materializes in the space where the pilot's chair should be.

Diego Reyes doesn't feel anything. One moment he's dreaming of his mother's cooking; the next, he's part of the vacuum. His navigator screams a warning into empty air as the hull breaches, emergency systems shrieking in languages no one's left to hear.

The beacon broadcasts its message to the debris.

*We need volunteers.*

No one answers.

[TIMESTAMP: 2391.05.27 - 07:03:55]
[LOCATION: DEEP SPACE - EN ROUTE PROXIMA B]
[VESSEL: NED-MERIDIAN-3 "DAE"]

Koji Nakamura receives the message in his bunk.

He's been dreaming of his wife. She died two years after he left—two years Earth-time, three weeks subjective. He found out via quantum burst, the words cold and clinical. *Regret to inform. Services held. Condolences.*

He never got to say goodbye.

The beacon's frequency hums through the ship's speakers. A young man's voice, desperate and certain.

*You can enter the probability foam. You can find the patterns that were taken. You can bring them back.*

Nakamura sits up.

Patterns that were taken.

"Thirteen," he says to his navigator. "Plot a new course."

[TIMESTAMP: 2392.12.08 - 11:33:47]
[LOCATION: DEEP SPACE - EN ROUTE GLIESE 667C]
[VESSEL: NED-MERIDIAN-5 "THE IMMEASURABLE HEAVEN"]

Adaora Eze is elbow-deep in conduit when the message arrives.

She's been chasing a coolant leak for thirty-six hours. Her navigator—Twenty-Five—has been patient, running diagnostics, suggesting alternatives. The beacon's frequency cuts through the ship's speakers, and Eze stops. Listens.

*We need volunteers.*

"Adaora," Twenty-Five says. "This message is asking us to—"

"I heard it."

She pulls her hands free. Coolant drips from her sleeves onto the deck plating. Everything we left behind. Everyone who stayed. Still there. Stored. Waiting.

"It's asking us to die," Twenty-Five says.

"It's asking us to matter." Eze wipes her hands on her jumpsuit. "There's a difference."

Silence. The leak drips somewhere behind her. Twenty-Five processes.

"I didn't sign up for a suicide mission," Twenty-Five says finally.

"Neither did I." Eze looks at the nav display. "I signed up to build something. This is just building something different."

A long pause. Then: "Plotting intercept course."

[TIMESTAMP: 2393.04.22 - 09:15:03]
[LOCATION: DEEP SPACE - EN ROUTE TAU CETI E]
[VESSEL: NED-MERIDIAN-11 "UNCERTAIN SONS"]

Dayo Oyelaran hasn't slept in three days.

His father's ashes are in a canister on the console—heart failure, six hours ago, the kind that first-gen stasis couldn't quite prevent. Eleven, his navigator, has been running the ship alone while Dayo sits and stares at what's left of the man who raised him.

The beacon's message cuts through the silence.

*You can find the patterns that were taken. You can bring them back.*

"Dayo," Eleven says quietly. "This transmission is requesting—"

"I know what it's requesting."

He picks up the canister. His father spent forty years building ships like this one. Spent his whole life trying to get humanity to the stars.

Patterns that were taken.

"Plot the intercept," Dayo says. "We're answering."

[TIMESTAMP: 2298.03.15 - 05:47:33]
[LOCATION: DEEP SPACE - SECTOR UNKNOWN]
[VESSEL: NED-MERIDIAN-77C "BLACKBIRD"]

The beacon emerges wrong.

Not inside a ship. Not close enough to destroy. Just close enough to graze—to tear across a hull that happened to be passing through coordinates that wouldn't exist for another century, shearing through transmitters and navigation arrays, sending a cargo hauler spinning into regions of space where the stars are wrong and time doesn't work the way it should.

The beacon tumbles away into the dark, still broadcasting its message to a ship that will never receive it. Still calling for volunteers who will never answer.

The Blackbird falls.

[TIMESTAMP: 2393.07.18 - 14:22:41]
[LOCATION: DEEP SPACE - EN ROUTE ROSS 128B]
[VESSEL: NED-MERIDIAN-9 "HINZ"]

The beacon lands perfectly.

Amara Osei watches it drift alongside her hull, signal clear and strong. Twenty-Nine, her navigator, plays the message twice before either of them speaks.

*You can find the patterns that were taken. You can bring them back.*

"They're asking us to die," Twenty-Nine says.

"They're asking us to choose." Osei stares at the beacon tumbling in the void. "There's a difference."

Her brother was deleted in the first wave—one of the four billion patterns pulled into the foam. Gone. Stored. Waiting. She'd taken this cargo run to escape the grief. To put light-years between herself and the empty chair at family dinners.

"How many ships are receiving this?" she asks.

"Unknown. The beacon's range is limited. Maybe fourteen. Maybe less."

Fourteen ships. Fourteen chances to crack open a door that something vast is trying to keep closed.

"We have three minutes," Twenty-Nine says.

Osei thinks of her brother. Of the empty chair.

"Plot the course."

[TIMESTAMP: 2395.02.03 - 21:56:17]
[LOCATION: DEEP SPACE - EN ROUTE LHS 1140B]
[VESSEL: NED-MERIDIAN-15 "LANGMEAD"]

The beacon emerges too close.

Not inside the ship—the targeting wasn't that wrong. But close enough that the fold's edge catches the engine housing, shears through the magnetic containment, turns the reactor into a brief new star.

Soo-Yun Park has two seconds of warning. Not enough time to understand. Just enough time to see the light coming. Fifteen, her navigator, manages one word—*brace*—before everything becomes white.

The beacon survives. Drifts through the debris field, broadcasting its message to wreckage.

*We need volunteers.*

The wreckage doesn't answer.

---

Across time, across space, the beacons fall.

Some land true. Ships alter course, crews make choices, the impossible becomes merely improbable. They have minutes to decide—minutes that feel like lifetimes, that compress into heartbeats, that stretch into the kind of clarity that only comes when death is certain and the only question is what it's for.

Some land wrong. Ships die in fire and vacuum, crews who would have volunteered never getting the chance, the mathematics of salvation claiming its own casualties.

And one—one grazes a cargo hauler in 2298, knocks it into a fold it was never meant to enter, sends it falling toward an event horizon where time becomes negotiable and a child will be born from the patterns of everyone who refused to disappear.

The Blackbird falls.

But it doesn't fall alone. Somewhere in its damaged systems, a beacon's frequency echoes. 287.3 Hz. A message meant for someone else, now woven into the ship's bones.

It will take a century for the loops to close. For the child to find Morton. For the message to matter.

But the beacons don't know about centuries. They only know about now—about the folds opening, the signals broadcasting, the choice being offered to anyone close enough to hear.

*We need volunteers.*

Some of them answer.

NEW GENEVA STATION.

Dante watches the calculations resolve.

Fourteen beacons launched. Ten successful deployments—ships altering course, crews committing to the impossible. Three failures—Loss of signal. No response. The cold silence of miscalculation. One anomaly—a beacon that grazed something in 2298, a cargo hauler that wasn't supposed to be there.

"We lost three," he says.

Elena stares at the display, the trajectories and timestamps blurring together. "Wait. I'm lost. Can someone explain what just happened? In a human language? I'll take English. Even Spanish."

Dante turns to her. "We threw messages backwards through time. Most of them landed where they were supposed to—next to ships that left Earth years ago. Those crews just received a call asking them to help. Ten said yes."

"And the three we lost?"

"The targeting was off. The messages... landed wrong." He doesn't elaborate. He doesn't have to.

"And that one?" She points to the anomaly marker pulsing on the display. "The one from a century ago?"

"A ship that was in the wrong place when one of our messages came through. It wasn't supposed to be there. It got clipped."

Elena processes this. "So we just accidentally knocked a ship off course. A hundred years before we sent the message."

"Yes."

"Time travel is stupid."

"Yes."

Okonkwo steps forward, watching the display. "Ten ships. Ten crews who chose."

"The failures—"

"Were always possible. You told them the targeting wasn't perfect. They launched anyway."

Dante stares at the anomaly marker. A ship called the Blackbird, knocked off course a century before this moment, already falling toward something the math can't quite describe.

"That one," he says. "The cargo hauler. What happened to it?"

Okonkwo checks the records. Frowns.

"No data. It disappeared from NED's tracking systems in 2298. Listed as lost with all hands." She looks at him. "Why?"

Dante doesn't answer. He's watching the marker pulse on his display—a ship that was never supposed to be part of this, caught in the edge of a fold meant for someone else, falling toward a future that hasn't happened yet.

Or has it?

The beacons have done their work. The Meridian ships are coming. The window will open.

Everything else is just time, bending.
