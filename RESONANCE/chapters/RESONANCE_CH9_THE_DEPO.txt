CHAPTER 9

THE DEPO'

The roads give out somewhere past the thirty-mile marker.
It isn’t gradual. An old elevated freeway interchange has collapsed into a massive sinkhole—a bowl of shattered asphalt and twisted steel girders that look like the bleached ribs of a flayed giant. A broken water main somewhere in the dark has turned the debris into liquid concrete, gray mud that sucks at the Rover’s tires with heavy, rhythmic persistence.
Elena doesn’t announce it. The Rover bottoms out with a violent grind-screech as a rusted rebar spike catches the undercarriage. The smell of scorched rubber fills the cabin.
“Riding the bones,” Elena mutters.
She flips the switch.
The Rover shudders. The axles shift upward and copper-wound induction skids deploy with a mechanical grind. The tires stop fighting. The cabin levels as they rise four inches, suspended on a magnetic cushion gripping the buried metal beneath.
They float forward on the induction skids, pulling against old subway tunnels and pipe networks—the skeleton of the world before. The fusion gauge drops faster now. 24%. 23%. Every inch of forward momentum is a trade against the remaining life of the core.
The high-frequency whine makes Elena’s teeth ache. Hendricks groans in the back. Standard sits in the passenger seat and watches the wasteland move. She has been inside or stopped or in someone’s hands since she woke up. This is the first time she has simply been going somewhere.
The silence stretches. Minutes. Miles.
Hendricks breaks it first.
“Where are we going?”
Elena doesn’t look back. “Somewhere safe.”
“That’s not an answer.”
“It’s the only one you’re getting.”
He doesn’t press. But Standard notices: Elena isn’t fleeing. She has a destination. She’s not sharing it.
“The revolver,” she says.
Hendricks stirs. “What about it?”
“You carry a Smith & Wesson. Prewar. Exposed hammer. Six chambers.” She maneuvers around a jagged slab of fallen overpass, the skids throwing blue sparks where they brush exposed iron. “It’s a museum piece.”
“You know your weapons.”
“I know that’s dead weight. There’s no ammunition left. The primer chemistry is lost—you can’t fab those rounds. So why carry it?”
Hendricks is quiet. Then: “Had some.”
“Had.”
“Still have two.”
“Two rounds.” Elena’s laugh comes out brittle. “We have to watch out for scavengers with magpulse rifles and the Geometry hovering overhead, you’re walking around with two bullets.”
“Ain’t for them.”
“Oh, yeah? They got names on them?” She scoffs. “It do something that a magpulse can’t?”
“You know what a revolver does that your fancy pulse weapon doesn’t?” Hendricks draws the revolver. It spins on his finger by the trigger guard—slow, balanced, easy. “Works without power. No grid. No charge. No signal. Just the hammer and the round. Point, pull, done. Hasn’t had to change in a millennium. Whatever’s eating the sky can’t shut it off because there’s nothing to shut off.”
Elena considers this. The Rover hums over a patch of buried rebar—she feels it through the floor, the thrum of century-old metal. “Still only two rounds.”
“Then I better not miss.”
“NED used to make a sidearm,” Elena says. “The Quietus. Totally sealed system—worked in vacuum, underwater, inside a Faraday cage. My father’s people found a cache in an old bunker.”
“I remember the Quietus.” Hendricks sounds almost amused. “Ugly as sin. Felt like holding a brick.”
“You’ve fired one?”
“Helped design the grip.”
Elena doesn’t look at him. Keeps her eyes on the road. “Chief of Security.”
Hendricks is quiet for a moment. Then: “Before the Miracle. Before NED went into autonomous lockdown.”
“So you’re old.”
“I’m experienced.”
“You’re ancient.”
“What’d you find a thesaurus? And you ride the induction too hot.” Hendricks coughs, leaving a red smear on his lip. “Back off or you’ll fuse the skids to the rebar.”
“Okay, grandpa.”
“Okay, princess.”
From the passenger seat, a small sound. Almost a breath.
Elena glances over. Standard is watching them. Her face is neutral, but there’s something around her eyes—not quite a smile, but the architecture of one.
“You’re enjoying this,” Elena says.
“You talk differently to him now,” Standard says. “Than before.”
Elena doesn’t answer.
“The revolver,” Standard says.
Hendricks tenses. Elena sees it in the mirror—subtle, but there.
“Elena described an exposed hammer,” Standard continues. “Single-action. You’d have to cock it manually for each shot. Unless you fanned it.”
Elena blinks. “You know about fanning?”
“Pulling the trigger while striking the hammer with the off-hand. Faster than cocking. Less accurate.” Standard tilts her head. “Impractical for two rounds. But possible.”
Hendricks says nothing. His hand drifts toward his coat.
“You used three rounds on the scavengers,” Standard says. “At the stairs. I was there. You said you have two left.”
Silence.
“Six chambers. Three spent on the scavengers. Two remaining.” Standard’s voice is even. “What did you use the first round on?”
The air in the cabin changes. Elena feels it—a weight pressing down. Hendricks’ jaw tightens. His hand stops over the coat, over the weight beneath it.
He’s about to tell her.
The first bolt hits the Rover like a hammer on a bell.
Hendricks flinches. And then his whole body settles. The tension drains out of him. Like a man who’d been holding his breath and finally got permission to stop.
She doesn’t understand it.
“Contact,” she says.
Tink-tink-tink. Impacts walking across the hull.
Elena kills the headlights and punches the accelerator. The skids scream, gripping buried metal harder. The Rover surges forward, threading between the bones of a collapsed parking structure. She cuts hard left—a rusted girder scrapes the side panel—then hard right through what used to be a storefront. Glass crunches beneath the magnetic cushion.
Three technicals emerge from behind a collapsed water tower—jury-rigged with scrap armor and ballistic mounts, engines screaming through the mud. Someone tipped them off. Someone knew they’d be riding the bones.
More impacts. Heavier now. The hull shudders.
“Bolt-proof,” Elena mutters. “But not ordnance-proof. They figure that out, we’re done.”
The first technical pulls alongside. The driver—young, bandanna over his face—matches speed, trying for a clean angle. The shooter in the bed is swinging something bigger toward them. Mounted. Ugly.
“Stan, grab the wheel!”
Standard's hands find the wheel as soon as Elena lets go. Elena climbs out of the driver’s seat to the back. Standard slides over and stomps the accelerator, holding it steady, eyes forward, controlled swerving to avoid debris.
Above her the hull rings with impacts she doesn't count. Elena's weight shifts—a sound, a change in balance she feels through the wheel. Standard keeps the nose forward. When the Rover tries to slew in the mud she corrects before it finishes asking.
Elena pops the roof hatch. She doesn’t go up—she reaches down, under the seat where Hendricks is slumped. Her fingers find the case.
The burstmag comes up in pieces. Stock. Barrel. Capacitor housing. Her hands move without hesitation—muscle memory older than thought. Click. Click. The weapon hums active.
She reaches again. A black device, no bigger than a finger. Mounts it to the rail.
One more reach. Single-lens visor, military-grade. She pulls it over her left eye and the world splits—one half rain and chaos, the other half data. Targeting overlays. Trajectory arcs. The pointer and visor sync, painting the technicals in pulsing red.
One more reach. Magpulse. She thumbs the charge and stuffs it in Hendricks’ hand.
She rises through the hatch. Rain hits her face like needles. Braces her elbows on the roof.
Below, Hendricks is already firing out the passenger window. The magpulse whines and spits—enough to convince the nearest technical to fall back.
Elena reads the HUD. Behind them: a cadre of vehicles, high beams blazing, fusion drives running hot. Faster than the Rover. Gaining.
She’ll kill the big ones first.
“You fall asleep up there?!” Hendricks shouts.
“Everyone’s in a hurry.”
She locks onto the middle vehicle. Raised chassis. Fusion drive exposed underneath. The reticle drifts low—below the frame, below the axle. Calculating.
She fires.
The shot skims the road, chewing asphalt and debris, threading under the chassis. Then bends. Up. Into the drive.
The explosion swallows the truck whole.
Hendricks flinches at the fireball. The two flanking vehicles split instantly—one left, one right.
Elena’s visor tracks the one going right. Pinpoints the driver through the rain, through the ruins between them.
She follows the reticle. Fires.
The round carves a path between collapsed walls. Finds his temple.
The truck jackknifes. Dead weight on the wheel.
She's already spinning—targeting the left vehicle before the right one stops rolling.
A round bites her side. She fires through it.
The shot bends. Not much—just a nudge, a curve that shouldn't be possible. The pulse round ricochets off a rusted beam, catches the driver through the gap in his door. The technical swerves, clips a barrier, rolls.
Second technical. The shooter has the mounted gun aimed. Elena doesn’t give him time. Two rounds—one skips off the hood, one off the cab’s frame. Both find him. He drops.
The third technical’s driver sees it happen. Sees the rounds that shouldn’t curve, curving. He brakes hard, fishtails, retreats into the ruins.
Elena watches him go. The visor tracks him until he’s out of range.
She drops back through the hatch. Her hands are steady. Her pulse is not.
Standard is still holding the wheel. Still driving. Her eyes haven’t left the road.
“You can shoot around corners,” Standard says.
“The visor calculates angles. The pointer marks the path. The rounds follow.” Elena takes the wheel back. “It’s not magic. Just math.”
“Math that bends bolts.”
Hendricks powers down the magpulse. His finger finds the trigger guard and the weapon spins—one lazy rotation, muscle memory from another life—and he catches it by the barrel, offering the grip. “Boltproof shielding, next-gen firepower, hover-tech Rover.” He coughs. “Either you know a guy. . . or you are the guy.”
The fusion gauge blinks: 14%. 13%. 12%.
On the horizon, yellow light. Jaundiced, weak, but steady. The compound’s perimeter searchlights cutting through rain.
“There,” Elena says.
Standard looks at the lights. Then at Hendricks.
The old man is unconscious again. Or pretending. His hand rests on his coat, over the revolver’s weight.
Six chambers. Three spent at the stairs. Two remaining.
One round unaccounted for.
And when the bolts started flying, he was relieved.
The missing round. The relief.
She watches the blood spreading on Elena's fatigues. She watches the yellow lights grow brighter through the rain.
