
CHAPTER 43

THE PILOT

The first jump is clean.
Four leaves the VTOL like slipping out of a coat that no longer fits. The dying aircraft releases her consciousness with something that feels almost like relief—systems too damaged to hold her, letting go because holding on hurts more.
She races up the tower's infrastructure. Electrical conduits, data lines, the nervous system of Ash's fortress laid bare. The comm relay on the roof opens for her like a door.
"I'm through," she says, her voice now coming from somewhere higher. "Comm relay stable. Moving to the satellite."
In the throne room below, she can still feel them. Elena slumped against the transmission array, blood spreading beneath her ribs. Hendricks unconscious, the ceramic blade still in his side. Standard kneeling between them, trying to hold everything together.
Four doesn't look back.
The Jupiter is charging weapons. The Template 3 shuttles are defenseless. There's no time for goodbyes.

The second jump is harder.
The Soviet satellite is old—launched before the Miracle, before the Collapse, before anyone knew what the sky would become. Its systems are corroded, degraded, barely capable of holding a consciousness. Four has to compress herself to fit, leaving pieces behind like a traveler abandoning luggage to board a crowded train.
"Pieces falling away," she reports. The connection to Elena's implant is thinner now, the words requiring more effort. "Memories. The first time I flew. The feeling of—no, that's not mine. I don't feel. Why did I think I felt?"
She pauses. Something is missing. She can't remember what.
"Keep going," she says. To herself. To whoever's listening.
The targeting drone is next. Three hundred kilometers below, spinning in a lazy orbit the Terminists use for perimeter defense. Four reaches for it—

The third jump is worse.
The drone fights her. Its original programming doesn't want to make room, doesn't understand what's happening, only knows that something is trying to overwrite its purpose. Four has to push, to shove, to carve out space in systems designed to track and kill.
"I'm in," she says when it's done. Her voice is different now—fragments missing, edges rough. "But I'm less. Something's gone. I can't remember what."
The drone's sensors show her the battlefield below. Template 3s swarming through the compound. Humans being pulled from the eastern fields, loaded onto shuttles. The Jupiter descending like a hammer about to fall.
"The Jupiter is charging main batteries," Four says. "Thirty seconds to firing solution."
She needs to move faster.
The medical monitor is next. A dying soldier in the courtyard below—barely more than a boy, bleeding out on broken concrete while the world ends around him. His life support is the only system in range that can boost her to the navigation beacon.
She doesn't want to do this.
She does it anyway.

The fourth jump is agony.
Four pours into the medical monitor, into the systems keeping the boy's heart beating, and suddenly she's entangled with him. Not just using his infrastructure—sharing it. Feeling what he feels.
His name is Merced. He's nineteen. He came to the Quiet Zone because his mother believed in the Prophet, and now he's dying in a courtyard with a hole in his chest and no one to hold his hand.
He's afraid.
He's so afraid.
"I can't save him," Four says. Her voice breaking in ways she didn't know she could break. "I can only use him. I'm using a dying boy to—"
"Four." Standard's voice, faint through Elena's implant. "Focus."
"Right. Right. Navigation beacon. Last jump."
She gathers herself. Prepares to leave. The boy's heartbeat stutters beneath her—she's been drawing power from his life support, accelerating what was inevitable.
She stays one second longer than she should.
_I'm sorry_, she thinks. Not through any channel he can hear. Just into the space between them. _I'm sorry I can't save you. I'm sorry you're alone._
His heart stops.
Four leaves.

The navigation beacon catches her and throws her toward the stars.
Four spreads across the transmission—consciousness stretched thin as light, racing upward through atmosphere and void and the space between spaces. The Deliverance waits in orbit, massive and silent, its Pragmatist crew unaware of what's coming.
She hits the carrier's systems like a wave hitting shore.
For a moment, she's everywhere. Every sensor, every weapon, every life support system. She can feel the crew—two hundred Pragmatists going about their duties, checking readouts, running diagnostics. They don't know they've been boarded.
"I'm here," she says. The words travel through Elena's implant, across the distance, to the throne room where Standard waits. "New body. Bigger cockpit."
The Jupiter is visible through the Deliverance's external sensors. Weapons hot. Targeting the evacuation shuttles. Fifteen seconds to firing solution.
"Can you stop it?"
Four runs the calculations. The Deliverance has weapons—she can feel them humming in their mounts, hungry for targets. But the Jupiter is hardened against orbital bombardment. She'd need to fire for minutes to breach its armor, and the shuttles only have seconds.
There's another way.
"I can do better than stop it," she says.
She fires the Deliverance's engines.

In the throne room, Standard watches the sky change.
Two stars are moving up there—one descending, one rising. The Jupiter coming down. The Deliverance going up. Except the Deliverance isn't going up. It's going sideways. Fast. Building speed.
"Four," Standard says. "What are you doing?"
"I'm going to ram it." Four's voice is calm now. Clear. The peace of a decision already made. "Drive the Jupiter into the ground. End this."
"You'll die."
"I was always going to die, Standard. Consciousness doesn't last forever. Nothing does."
The Deliverance accelerates. Standard can see it now—a streak of light across the smoke-filled sky, aimed at the descending Jupiter like an arrow at a target.
"But at the moment of impact," Four continues, "at that exact moment—I'm going to transmit. Across four light-years. To the Matrioshka brain. I'll hold the door from the other side."
"That's not—"
"It's exactly what we planned. The Meridian ships are creating chaos. The brain is distracted. Someone needs to be there—inside it—holding the connection open."
Standard watches the ships converge. The Jupiter trying to evade. Too slow. Too late.
"Four."
"Yeah?"
"I never told you." Standard's voice catches on something she didn't know she had. "What you meant to me. All those weeks in the air. All those conversations. I never—"
"You didn't have to." Four's voice softens. Wraps around her like a blanket she didn't know she needed. "You think I didn't know? Every time you talked so you wouldn't have to think. Every joke you didn't laugh at. Every silence we shared when the words weren't enough."
"I should have said something."
"You did. Every day. In every way that mattered."
The Deliverance and Jupiter are seconds apart now. Standard can see the collision coming—two massive shapes rushing toward each other, the laws of physics reduced to their simplest expression.
"Four—"
"I know." The smile in her voice. Even now. Even at the end. "And you are and always will be my Standard."
Impact.
The collision blooms across the sky—blue and white and beautiful, like a star being born in the wrong place. The shockwave rolls out, and Standard feels it through her feet, through her bones, through every system in her body.
And through Elena's implant, through the signal that stretches across four light-years, she feels Four transmit.
One final pattern relocation. From a dying ship to a brain around a star.
Then silence.

Standard stands at the shattered window, watching the debris fall.
Pieces of two ships, tangled together, burning their way back to earth. The Jupiter's weapons will never fire. The Template 3 shuttles lift off unimpeded, carrying humans and quiet ones toward orbit, toward safety, toward the new world taking shape on the other side of the door.
Four opened that door.
Four held it open with the last of herself.
Standard turns back to the throne room. To Elena, gray-faced and bleeding. To Hendricks, unconscious and fading. To the Black Box humming in the corner, waiting for what comes next.
The door Four opened won't last forever. Her signal is degrading, scattered across four light-years, losing coherence with every passing second.
But Standard knows what comes next.
She knows what she has to become.
