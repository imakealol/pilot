CHAPTER 10

SHINE DOWN

The fusion gauge dies at sunset.
Not a gradual fade—a hard stop. The number blinks from 3% to ERROR to nothing, and the Rover shudders once, twice, and goes silent. The induction skids cut out. They drop four inches onto broken asphalt, and the impact rattles Standard’s teeth.
“Shit.” Elena slams her palm against the dashboard. “Shit, fuck, god damnit!”
The road ahead is empty. Behind them, the same. Gray dust and skeletal trees and the black bars overhead, drinking the last of the light.
Hendricks stirs in the back. He’s been drifting in and out for the last hundred miles, his breathing wet and wrong. The bandage on his neck has soaked through again.
“How far?” His voice is gravel.
“I don’t know.” Elena is already out of the Rover, scanning the horizon. “There was supposed to be a waystation.”
“How do you know that?” Hendricks asks.
“Through the Terminist network—supply route.”
She feels a hot stare.
“I paid one for the Intel. What? You think I was going to drive across the Depo’ with a wish and a prayer? I do research.”
“Well, I’d check your sources.”
“It’s there. It has to be there.”
Standard steps out. The air is cold and thin, tasting of dust and something chemical—old batteries, maybe, or the residue of fires that burned out years ago. She looks west, where the sun is bleeding into the black bars, and she sees it.
Light.
Not much. A flicker, low on the horizon. But steady.
“There,” she says.
Elena follows her gaze. Her shoulders drop—not relief, just the release of one tension to make room for another.
“That’s it. The Array.” She turns back to the Rover. “We push.”
They push.
Hendricks steers, occasionally giving unhelpful advice about pushing the right way.
The Rover weighs more than it should. The Black Box in the cargo bed, the supplies, the dying man in the back seat—all of it drags against the broken road. Standard takes one side, Elena the other, and they lean into it, boots scraping, muscles burning.
Standard’s hands scream. The wounds from the box have scabbed over, but the pressure reopens them, and she feels the wet heat of fresh blood in her palms. She doesn’t stop. Stopping means dying. She knows this without knowing how she knows it.
The light grows closer. Resolves into shapes. A chain-link fence topped with solar panels—hundreds of them, tilted toward the southern sun during the day. Like flowers that forgot how to bloom. Behind the fence, structures. Low buildings. Movement.
People.
A man steps out of the gate. He’s holding a rifle—not a burstmag, something older, projectile-based. He watches them approach with the flat patience of someone who has seen this before.
“Rover’s dead,” Elena calls out. “We need a charge.”
“Everyone needs a charge.” The man doesn’t lower the rifle. “What do you have to trade?”
Elena reaches into her jacket. Pulls out a chip—one of the last, Standard knows, because she watched Elena count them at the outpost before leaving most of it for the woman who gave her Otis's clothes. She holds it up.
The man looks at it. Looks at the Rover. Looks at Elena’s armor, at Standard’s bloody hands, at the shape in the back seat that might be a body.
“That’ll get you a trickle,” he says. “Twelve hours. Maybe enough to limp to the next station, if there is one.”
“We’ll take it.”
The gate opens.
---
The Array is a graveyard pretending to be a town.
The solar panels are old—pre-Miracle tech, scratched and faded, maybe forty percent efficiency on a good day. But there are acres of them. The buildings beneath them are repurposed shipping containers, same as the outpost, but these are arranged in a circle around a central structure: a squat concrete bunker with cables snaking out of it like veins.
The power hub. The heart they’re all feeding from.
People move between the containers. Not many—maybe thirty, forty. They’re thin. Watchful. They look at the Rover the way you look at a wound that might be infected.
Elena parks near the charging station. A woman in oil-stained coveralls hooks up the cables, and the dashboard flickers—a slow, patient pulse that means the trickle has started.
“Twelve hours,” the woman says. “Don’t drain anything else or you’ll be here for twenty.”
"Yes, ma'am." Elena feels she's the type to appreciate being "ma'am'd."
Standard helps Hendricks out of the back. He can barely stand. His weight sags against her, and she feels the heat coming off him—fever, maybe, or just the slow fire of a body burning itself for fuel.
“He needs medical attention,” she says.
The woman in coveralls looks at Hendricks. At the blood on his collar. At the way his legs won’t hold him.
“Kellerman,” she says. Not to them—to someone behind her. “Got another one.”
A man emerges from one of the containers.
He’s older than Elena, younger than Hendricks. It was always hard to tell just based on appearances with people living in the Deposit. Dark hair going gray at the temples. A face that might have been handsome before exhaustion carved it into something sharper. He moves with the careful efficiency of someone who has learned not to waste motion.
He’s carrying a medical kit. Old. Battered. Real.
“Bring him inside,” he says.
---
The container has been converted into a clinic. Cots line the walls—most empty, a few occupied by shapes that don’t move much. The smell is antiseptic and copper and something cloying underneath, the smell of bodies fighting battles they’re losing.
Standard lowers Hendricks onto a cot. He groans, then goes quiet, his eyes half-closed.
The doctor—Kellerman—pulls up a stool. He doesn’t ask questions yet. He just works. Gloves on. Penlight in Hendricks’ eyes. Fingers on his pulse. The bandage on his neck, peeled back with a gentleness that surprises Standard.
“Regulator withdrawal,” Kellerman says. Not a question.
“Yes,” Standard says.
“How long since removal?”
“I don’t know. Days.”
Kellerman looks at the wound. The edges are inflamed, angry red. The hole where the node used to be is trying to heal around something that isn’t there anymore.
“He did this himself,” Kellerman says.
“Yes.”
“Brave or stupid.”
“I don’t know the difference.”
He reaches into his kit, pulls out a vial, loads a syringe.
“This will help with the fever. The withdrawal—” He shakes his head. “That’s just time. And luck.”
He administers the injection. Hendricks flinches, then settles. His breathing evens out, just slightly.
Kellerman’s hands are steady. Precise. He rebandages the wound with the same careful efficiency, and Standard watches his fingers work—the way they move, the way they know where to go without looking.
Hendricks is watching too.
His eyes are slits, barely open, but they’re tracking. Not the ceiling. Not Standard.
Kellerman.
The doctor finishes the bandage. He looks up, and for a moment their eyes meet—Hendricks on the cot, Kellerman on the stool. Something passes between them. Standard doesn’t have a name for it. She just knows she’s not supposed to be seeing it.
Kellerman looks away first.
“Rest,” he says. “I’ll check on you in a few hours.”
He stands. Peels off the gloves. Turns to Standard.
His eyes move over her—the ruined hands, the borrowed clothes, the stillness that isn’t quite right. He frowns, the way you frown at a puzzle with missing pieces.
“You’re not injured,” he says.
“No.”
“You pushed that Rover a quarter mile. In this air. After—” He gestures at her hands. “That.”
“Yes.”
He waits for more. Standard doesn’t give it.
“There’s food in the common area,” he says finally. “You should eat. Both of you.”
He leaves.
Standard looks at Hendricks. His eyes are closed now, but there’s something different in his face. Softer. Or maybe just more human.
---
The common area is another container, this one fitted with benches and a long table and a heating unit that radiates a thin, desperate warmth. A few people sit in clusters, eating from dented bowls. They look up when Elena enters. They look longer when Standard follows.
Elena sits. A woman brings two bowls—some kind of stew, gray-brown, the smell of it triggering something in Standard’s chest. Not hunger. Recognition. She knows what food is. She knows what eating is.
She doesn’t feel the need.
Elena eats like she’s dying. Head down, spoon moving, not stopping to breathe. She’s been running on nutrition capsules since NED, and her body is demanding payment.
Standard sits across from her. The bowl steams in front of her. She looks at it.
“Eat,” Elena says between mouthfuls. “We don’t know when we’ll get another chance.”
Standard picks up the spoon. The metal is cold in her fingers. She dips it into the stew, lifts it toward her mouth. Warmth, salt, the slick of fat coating her tongue—all of it registers, and she feels it go down to her stomach.
She doesn’t want it. That’s not quite right. She doesn’t need it. The way Elena needs it, the way Hendricks needed the injection—that desperate biological imperative. It isn’t there. She can smell the food. She can see it, even taste it. But it doesn’t connect to anything inside her.
She puts the spoon down.
Elena doesn’t notice. She’s already scraping the bottom of her bowl, looking for more.
But someone else does.
Kellerman is standing in the doorway. He’s watching Standard. Watching the untouched bowl. Watching the way she holds the spoon like she’s never held one before, or like she’s forgotten why anyone would.
He walks over.
“Not hungry?”
“No.”
“When did you last eat?”
Standard thinks. The apartment. The water Hendricks gave her. But that was water, not food. Before that—
Nothing. The box. The dark. And before that, the blankness that swallows everything.
“I don’t remember.”
Kellerman’s frown deepens. He pulls out a penlight—the same one he used on Hendricks. “May I?”
Standard doesn’t understand the question. She doesn’t move.
He takes it as permission. He shines the light in her eyes. Checks her pulse. His fingers are warm on her wrist, and she feels the pressure, but it’s distant. Clinical.
“Pupils responsive. Pulse is...” He pauses. Counts again. “Slow. Very slow.”
“Is that wrong?”
“It’s unusual.” He releases her wrist. “Have you been feeling faint? Weak?”
“No.”
“No nausea? Headaches?”
“No.”
He’s looking at her differently now. The puzzle has added pieces, but they don’t fit.
“She pushed the Rover,” he says. Louder now. Not to her—to the room.
Heads turn.
“A quarter mile,” he continues. “Uphill. In thin air. With those hands.” He nods at her bloody palms. “And she’s not eating. She’s not tired. Her resting heart rate is forty beats per minute.”
The silence in the container changes. Thickens.
“Doc.” One of the men at the table stands. He’s big, thick through the shoulders, with a face that’s seen violence and learned to expect it. “What are you saying?”
“I’m saying I don’t know what she is.”
The words hang in the air.
Elena has stopped eating. She wishes she didn’t leave her magpulse in the Rover.
“She’s with me,” Elena says. “She’s human.”
“Is she?” The big man is walking closer. Others are standing now, moving with him. “Because my brother was human. And he dropped. My wife was human. She dropped too. Everyone dropped.” He stops three feet from Standard. “Everyone except the machines.”
“I’m not—” Standard starts.
“Then eat.”
He grabs her bowl. Shoves it toward her. Stew slops over the edge, splashing her borrowed shirt.
“Eat,” he says again. “Prove it.”
Standard looks at the bowl. At the stew dripping down her chest. At the faces around her—scared, angry, desperate. Looking for someone to blame.
She picks up the spoon.
She puts food in her mouth.
She chews vegetables and meat.
She can sense the flavors, but it changes nothing. No need fulfilled, no energy revived. Like texture without meaning. She swallows, and it sits in her chest like a stone, and she knows—without knowing how she knows—that her body doesn’t want this. Doesn’t need this. Will reject it if she forces more.
“More,” the man says.
She takes another spoonful. Another.
The stone in her chest grows heavier.
“That’s enough.” Kellerman steps between them. “She’s eating. She’s clearly unwell. Pushing her isn’t going to prove anything.”
“It proves plenty.” The man isn’t backing down. “It proves she ain’t right. It proves she’s one of them—the ones the sky left standing. The ones who did this to us.”
“She didn’t do anything—”
“They never do. They just stand there. They just watch.” He’s shouting now. “They watch us die and they don’t even blink!”
The container erupts. Voices overlapping, accusations flying. Someone grabs Standard’s arm. Someone else shoves Elena back.
Standard doesn’t fight. She doesn’t run.
She just stands there, the stew turning to acid in her throat, and watches the fear become something uglier.
And outside, beyond the solar panels, beyond the fence, the black bars in the sky drift over the solar fields.
