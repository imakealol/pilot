CHAPTER 5

THE QUEUE

Three seats behind her sit empty. Goff. Otis. Aikin. Gone. Left in three different places in NED—Goff in the lobby, Otis at the checkpoint, Aikin at the end of it—while she drove away with their mission and without their bodies. Quiet Zone, last names only. She didn't know Goff's first name, didn't know Aikin's. Otis she'd picked up two days out, water rations her only theology. That was supposed to make it easier.
It doesn't.
Behind her, in the cargo bed, the Black Box hums.
A pressure. A vibration at 287.3 Hz that settles into the back of her molars and makes the scar on her palm itch. Sixteen thousand, seven hundred and forty-nine souls, screaming in harmony. A coffin full of ghosts she promised to deliver to her father.
"Shut up," she mutters.
The Box doesn't shut up.
Outside, the Depo is a riot of desperate biology. Tents cling to the base of the seawalls like barnacles. Neon signs, half of them burned out, bleed pink and green light into the rain. People—thousands of them—press toward the Static Line, a river of wet wool and desperation flowing toward the only gate that matters. The sorting pens run both sides of the approach road: chain-link cages where the augmented wait for processing that never comes. She doesn't look at the pens. She never looks.
Elena pulls into the Priority lane. Kills the engine. The silence inside the cab is sudden and heavy, broken only by the hum of the Box.
She has a problem.
Her implant. Dormant for eleven years, screaming since NED—live with Morton's data clusters, active and broadcasting like a confession. The checkpoint scanner will find it. The Terminists were built for exactly this.
She'd planned for Aikin to absorb their attention—obvious tech, wrist deck, sealed ports, a lightning rod. She'd slip through clean in his wake.
She shot Aikin in the back before the checkpoint.
Her thumb finds the scar on her palm. She presses until the old pain pushes out the new ones.
A pulse at the base of her skull. Three short beats. Control, checking in.
<The scanner will flag your implant, daughter. You need cover before you pull forward.>
She doesn't speak—just thinks at it, the way she's done since Ash gave her access. _Working on it._
_The queue has resources. Run an active scan. Look for something useful._
She opens the scanner and sweeps the crowd. Five thousand people resolve into data—a sea of low-grade signatures, background noise, the hum of the unaugmented living in a world that never shuts up. Medical implants. Old Regulator frequencies. Black market tech going cold.
She almost misses it.
There.
A hole in the data. Not low signal. Absence. Where there should be noise, there is nothing. A woman-shaped void standing near the concrete barrier forty meters out, watching the guards with the flat attention of someone who is very good at waiting. An old man beside her, leaning against the barrier like his legs have decided not to participate. He's shaking.
_I see it._
<A null signature,> Control says. <No network presence. No augment signal. Her absence can swallow your hot implant at the scanner—walk her through beside you and the system finds nothing anomalous. They're trying to hitch a ride. This solves both problems, daughter.>
Elena studies the null. The old man has something—faint, degraded, the ghost of a Regulator removed recently. Deep in withdrawal. That's what the shaking is.
She looks at the three empty seats behind her.
_Tactical,_ she tells herself. _This is purely tactical._
She opens the door.

***

The rain hits her face. She moves along the barrier and thirty meters out she feels it—a drop. Sudden vertigo, like stepping off a curb that isn't there. The implant at the base of her skull recoils. A hard, physical flinch.
Then silence.
Absence. Like going deaf. Like something has been removed from the air.
She stops three feet from the woman. Up close, the absence is nauseating—a magnetic wrongness, a hole where there should be signal. The woman is dark-haired, wet with rain, watching Elena with eyes that are clear and completely unreadable.
Elena doesn't let herself hesitate.
"You're not making this checkpoint," she says. "Neither is he." Not a threat—just weather. "I have a Priority Rover and I need to make Detroit. You ride with me, you get through."
The old man looks at the Rover. He looks at her armor. He calculates.
"We can't pay," he says.
"Didn't ask."
The woman studies Elena. Something is running behind those eyes, some internal calibration Elena can't read. Then it settles.
"Why," she asks as statement.
"You're useful to me," Elena says. "That's all you need."
The woman considers this. Then: "We accept."
Elena tips her chin at the Rover. "I need a name for the manifest."
The old man starts, "Her name is—"
"Standard." The woman looks at her steadily. "My name is Standard."
Elena blinks once, looks Hendricks' way. He turns away.
"Modest," she says, rolling her eyes. "And let me guess. His name is sub-standard."
Half a hesitation. Elena registered it. Standard says, "Swanstrom. Swanstrom Hendricks."
The old man grunts and sputters, "Just. . . just 'Hendricks.'"
_They're so weird._
They board. The old man collapses against the door. Standard settles in the seat beside him, eyes going immediately to the cargo bed—the heavy shape under its gray tarp—and then away.
Elena climbs in. Shuts out the rain. Reaches for Control—
Nothing.
She taps the implant. Checks the signal. The three-beat rhythm that has been there since she was fourteen, since Ash first trusted her with access—gone. The presence at the base of her skull: gone. She hadn't known how much space it took up until the space was empty.
She looks in the rearview mirror. Standard sits in the back, watching the queue through the rain-blurred glass. Unreadable. The absence radiates off her like cold.
The explanation offers itself readily enough: the null signature covers the signal the same way it will cover Elena's implant at the checkpoint. Same effect, ahead of time.
Elena doesn't know whether to be grateful.
The Box hums behind her. Three seats no one is sitting in. Two strangers she doesn't understand and a checkpoint she has to clear alone.
She puts the Rover in gear.
