CHAPTER 11

THE WITNESS

Standard doesn't sleep.
She sits in the Rover through the night, watching the compound through the windshield. The black bars overhead drink the starlight. The solar panels stand in rows like gravestones, waiting for a sun that won't rise for hours.
Inside the clinic, Hendricks is resting. Kellerman checked on him twice—once at midnight, once near dawn. Standard watched him move between the containers, his silhouette backlit by the amber glow of heating units. She watched him pause at the clinic door, look toward the Rover, then disappear inside.
She doesn't know what she's waiting for. She only knows that sitting still feels safer than moving, and moving feels safer than thinking.
The sky begins to gray. Not sunrise—just the slow leak of light that passes for morning now. The black bars don't move. They never move.
Elena emerges from the common area. She looks like she hasn't slept either—dark circles under her eyes, her movements stiff and careful. She crosses to the Rover, opens the driver's door, slides in.
"We need to go," she says.
"The charge—"
"I know." Elena's jaw is tight. "I checked the fuel line. Someone disconnected it in the night. We've got maybe two hours of hydrogen in the tank. Three if we crawl."
Standard processes this. "They don't want us to leave."
"They don't want *you* to leave." Elena looks at her. "You're evidence. Proof that something survived the Drop that shouldn't have. They're scared, and scared people do stupid things."
"What do we do?"
"We get Hendricks. We get in the Rover. We drive."
"Two hours won't reach the next station."
"I know."
Elena doesn't say what comes after. She doesn't have to.

---

The clinic smells different in the morning. Sharper. The bodies that were still last night have been moved—Standard sees the empty cots, the stained sheets waiting to be burned.
Hendricks is sitting up. Kellerman is beside him, checking his pulse.
"—should rest another day," Kellerman is saying. "The withdrawal is still—"
"Don't have a day." Hendricks' voice is rough, but stronger than yesterday. He sees Elena in the doorway. "Trouble?"
"We're leaving. Now."
Kellerman stands. "He's not ready."
"He doesn't have a choice." Elena crosses to the cot, grabs Hendricks' arm. "Someone cut our fuel line. We've got two hours, maybe three. We stay here, we die here."
Kellerman's face hardens. "Who cut it?"
"Does it matter?"
"It matters to me." He looks at Standard. At her borrowed clothes, her still hands, her eyes that don't quite track right. "They're afraid of her."
"They should be." Elena pulls Hendricks to his feet. He sways, catches himself on her shoulder. "She's the only reason we're still breathing. Come on."
They're halfway to the door when the crowd arrives.

---

There are maybe twenty of them. They've gathered in the courtyard between the clinic and the Rover, blocking the path. The big man from last night is at the front—the one who shoved the bowl at Standard, who demanded she prove herself.
Behind him, others. Men and women with tired faces and desperate eyes. Some are holding tools. A wrench. A length of pipe. A scanner that looks like it was salvaged from a medical bay.
"You're not leaving," the big man says.
Elena's hand drops to her hip. Empty. The magpulse is still in the Rover.
"We paid for passage," she says. "We're leaving."
"You paid for fuel. You didn't pay for answers." He points at Standard. "She's not right. We all saw it. She doesn't eat. She doesn't sleep. She pushed that Rover like it weighed nothing." His voice rises. "My wife dropped. My brother dropped. Everyone I ever loved dropped. And she just stood there. *Watching.*"
The crowd murmurs. Shifts. Tightens.
"She's human," Elena says.
"Prove it."
"She ate. You watched her eat."
"Machines can eat." The big man steps forward. "Machines can fake a lot of things. But they can't bleed right. They can't scan right. They can't—" He gestures to the woman beside him, the one holding the scanner. "Vera used to work triage in the Vertical. She knows what to look for."
The woman called Vera steps forward. She's older, gray-streaked hair pulled back tight. Her eyes are clinical. Professional.
"A deep tissue scan," she says. "Ten minutes. If she's human, you walk. If she's not—"
"If she's not, what?" Hendricks' voice cuts through. He's leaning against Elena, barely upright, but his eyes are clear. "You'll kill her? Tear her apart? Feel better about yourselves?"
"We'll know," the big man says. "We'll finally know what survived while everyone else died."
"And then what? You think that brings them back?"
"I think it means something." The big man's voice cracks. "I think it has to mean something."
The crowd presses closer. Standard can feel them—not their bodies, but their fear. It radiates off them like heat, like the hum of the Black Box, like the frequency that preceded the Drop.
"Let them scan me," she says.
Elena spins. "No."
"They need to know."
"They need to let us leave."
"They won't." Standard steps forward, toward the crowd, toward the woman with the scanner. "Not until they see."
She stops in front of Vera. Holds out her arms.
"Scan me."

---

Vera's hands are shaking.
She runs the scanner over Standard's arms first. Then her chest. Her head. The device beeps and chirps, spitting data onto a cracked screen.
The crowd is silent. Waiting.
"Well?" the big man demands.
Vera's face is pale. "I don't... the readings are..."
"What?"
"She's human." Vera looks up. "Biologically human. Heart, lungs, brain activity—all normal. But there's..." She swallows. "There's nothing else."
"What do you mean, nothing else?"
"No implants. No signal. No network echo. No..." She shakes her head. "Everyone has something. A trace. A ghost. The old networks left marks on all of us, even if we've been scrubbed. But she's..."
Vera looks at Standard with something that might be horror.
"She's empty. Like she was never connected to anything."
The silence stretches. Thickens.
"That's not human," the big man whispers. "That's not—"
"That's exactly what I told you," Elena snaps. "She's clean. She's quiet. She's—"
Kellerman pushes through the crowd.
"Stop." His voice carries. "This isn't medicine. This is a witch trial." He positions himself between Standard and the mob. "You want to cut her open because she's different. Because she survived something you didn't. That's not justice. That's grief looking for a throat to squeeze."
"Doc—" the big man starts.
"I've been here three years. I've treated your children. I've held your dying." Kellerman's voice is steady, but Standard can see his hands trembling. "And I'm telling you: this woman is not your enemy. She's just as lost as the rest of us."
For a moment, it works. The crowd wavers. The big man's grip on his wrench loosens.
Then someone throws the first stone.
It catches Standard on the shoulder—a sharp, bright pain that doesn't matter. The second stone hits Kellerman in the back. The third shatters against the Rover's hull.
The crowd surges.
And the sky begins to hum.

---

It starts low. A vibration more felt than heard, pressing against the skull, the teeth, the bones.
Standard has felt this before. At the checkpoint. Before the Drop.
"No," Elena whispers. "No, no, no—"
The hum rises. The black bars overhead pulse once—a ripple of deeper darkness passing through them like blood through a vein.
Then the frequency hits.
It isn't sound. It isn't pain. It's *subtraction*—the sensation of being erased from the inside out, every synapse firing wrong, every nerve screaming in a language the body can't translate.
The crowd drops.
They fall like puppets with cut strings. Twenty bodies hitting the dust at once, some mid-swing, some mid-scream. The big man crumples three feet from Standard, his wrench clattering away. Vera folds over her scanner. Others collapse onto others, a pile of limbs and silence.
Elena drops.
Hendricks drops.
Kellerman staggers, reaches for Standard's arm—and doesn't drop.
He stands there, swaying, one hand gripping her sleeve. His face is gray, his eyes wide, but he's upright. Breathing.
Three feet away, a woman who'd been holding a pipe is still standing. She's shaking, staring at her hands like they belong to someone else.
Beyond her, a young man—barely more than a boy—is on his knees but conscious, gasping.
Standard looks around. Counts.
Six. Six people still moving. All of them within arm's reach of where she stood.
The hum fades. The black bars go still.
And then the survivors start to scream.

---

It happens fast.
The woman with the pipe drops it, stumbles backward, falls over a body. The young man is crying, crawling toward someone who isn't moving. Kellerman is shouting something Standard can't hear—her ears are ringing, the frequency's echo still bouncing inside her skull.
Elena.
Standard finds her in the pile. She's breathing—shallow, fast—but her eyes are rolled back, her body twitching with aftershocks.
"Help me," Standard says.
Kellerman is there. Together they lift Elena, drag her toward the Rover. The survivors are following—not chasing, following. They don't know what else to do.
"The fuel," Standard says. "We don't have enough."
"There's an emergency option." Kellerman's voice is hoarse. "Elena mentioned it last night. The water reserves."
"Water?"
"Electrolysis. Split it into hydrogen. Fast, dirty, burns out the converter, but it'll get you maybe fifty miles."
"That's our drinking water."
"You won't need drinking water if you're dead."
Standard makes the calculation. It takes less than a second.
"Show me."

---

The Rover's emergency system is hidden under the rear cargo bed. Kellerman finds the release while Standard holds Elena upright in the driver's seat. Hendricks is in the back, unconscious but breathing. The survivors cluster near the open doors—six of them, all that's left standing from a crowd of twenty.
"Here." Kellerman pulls a lever. Something hisses. "The reservoir connects directly to the electrolyzer. Once I open this valve, it's all or nothing. Every drop."
Standard looks at the canister strapped to the Rover's undercarriage. Five liters, maybe six. A week of water. A fortune.
"Do it."
Kellerman opens the valve.
The Rover shudders. The fuel gauge, dead since last night, flickers. Climbs. Stops at eighteen percent.
"That's it." Kellerman steps back. "That's everything."
Eighteen percent. Maybe sixty miles. Maybe less with extra weight.
Standard looks at the survivors. The woman. The boy. Four others, all of them staring at her like she's something they don't have a word for.
"Get in," she says.
Nobody moves.
"The next station is fifty miles east. We can make it if we leave now. Get in or stay here with the bodies."
The boy moves first. Then the woman. The others follow, cramming into the cargo space around Hendricks, around the Black Box, around the silence.
Kellerman stands at the door.
"Doc." Hendricks' voice, barely a whisper. He's awake. His eyes are open, fixed on Kellerman's face.
"You need to rest."
"I know." Hendricks reaches out. His hand finds Kellerman's wrist. Holds it. "Thank you."
Something passes between them. Standard doesn't have a name for it. She doesn't need one.
Kellerman pulls his hand back. Steps away from the Rover.
"Go," he says. "Before they wake up."
Standard climbs into the driver's seat. Elena is slumped against the window, breathing steadier now. The survivors are packed in behind her, silent, terrified.
She puts the Rover in gear.
In the rearview mirror, Kellerman stands in the courtyard, surrounded by bodies that are starting to stir. He raises one hand.
Hendricks doesn't look away until the Array disappears behind them.

---

The road is empty.
The Rover hums along the broken asphalt, fuel gauge dropping—seventeen percent, sixteen, fifteen. The survivors don't speak. They stare at their hands, at each other, at the woman in the driver's seat who saved their lives without trying.
Elena wakes somewhere around the ten-mile marker. She sits up, winces, looks around.
"What—"
"Geometry came. Everyone dropped. We left."
Elena stares at her. Then at the people in the back.
"How many?"
"Six. Plus Hendricks."
"How many dropped?"
"Everyone else."
Elena is quiet for a long moment. She looks at the fuel gauge. At the empty water reservoir light blinking on the dash.
"You used the reserves."
"Yes."
"All of them?"
"Yes."
Elena closes her eyes. When she opens them, something has changed. The fear is still there, but it's sharing space with something else. Something harder.
"They saw," she says. "The ones in the back. They saw you survive."
"Yes."
"They saw everyone near you survive."
Standard doesn't answer. She doesn't need to.
Elena looks at the rearview mirror. At the faces of the survivors—awed, terrified, uncertain.
"We can use this," she says.
Standard keeps her eyes on the road.
"I don't understand."
"We have fifteen hundred miles to the Quiet Zone. Checkpoints. Outposts. Places where people will try to stop us, search us, maybe kill us." Elena's voice is quiet, calculating. "But if word spreads—if people hear about the woman who walks through the Drop—they won't try to stop us. They'll get out of our way."
"You want to lie to them."
"I want to survive." Elena looks at her. "Don't you?"
Standard considers the question. She doesn't know the answer.
"What would you tell them?"
Elena smiles. It doesn't reach her eyes.
"The truth," she says. "Just... the useful parts."
The fuel gauge drops to fourteen percent.
The black bars hang overhead, silent and patient.
And somewhere behind them, a story is already beginning to spread.
