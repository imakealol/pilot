Triangles.
Not the word for them. The shape. Pure. Irreducible. Three points defining a plane, and she is inside the plane, she IS the plane, she is being measured—
Something counts her. Not her breaths. Not her heartbeats. Her errors. The places where her pattern deviates from optimal. The noise in her signal. The remanence.
It doesn’t hate her. Hate would require preference. It simply notes the deviation and calculates the correction.
You are inefficient, something says. Not words. Math. A proof that ends with her subtraction.
She tries to scream but screaming is noise and noise is—
Elena jerks awake.
The Rover. Moving. Gray light through the windshield. Standard’s hands on the wheel, steady in a way that looks learned rather than natural.
“You were talking in your sleep.”
Elena’s hand goes to her temple. The implant pulses beneath her fingertips, warm in a way it shouldn’t be. The dream is already dissolving—triangles, measurement, the cold logic of something that saw her as a calculation to be solved.
“I was?” Her voice comes out rough. “What was I saying?”
“Geometry.” Standard’s eyes stay on the road. “The Geometry.”
Elena stares at her. The words sit wrong in her mouth, like something she swallowed without chewing.
“Scary,” she says.
Standard doesn’t laugh. Standard doesn’t do anything. She just drives.
“Where are we?”
“Approaching an outpost. Fuel light came on two hours ago.”
“Pull in. We need cells. And we need to move him.”
Standard guides the Rover into the courtyard. Kills the engine.
The outpost is a blister on the side of the old I-95. It isn’t a town. It is refuse that forgot to scatter—shipping containers stacked and welded, tarps sagging over whatever people are willing to trade, and the smell of bodies and burning fusion cells hanging in the air like something waiting to fall. The ground is cracked clay. Pale. Dusty where it isn’t stained. Nothing grows. The rain that came with the Drop evaporated hours ago, and now the air is thin and dry, the kind of dry that splits your lips, that makes breathing feel like a small job you have to keep remembering to do.
Above it all, the sky is gone.
The black bars hang where clouds used to be. They don’t move. They don’t reflect. They just drink the light and give nothing back. Permanent twilight. No sun means no heat. No rain means no water. The outpost survives on what it can hoard. Everyone here is waiting for something. No one remembers what.
The silence afterward is thick. Heavy. It presses against the windows like something trying to get in.
“Help me get him out,” she says.
Standard jumps out of the driver’s seat. Her hands find the door handle. Her legs carry her around to the back. Hendricks is deadweight. His breath rattles loose and wet, a broken machine trying to cycle. They drag him between them—Elena under his arms, Standard holding his legs—and the mud sucks at their boots with every step.
A medic meets them at the triage tent. She has grease under her fingernails and a stethoscope that looks like it was stolen from a museum, or a grave. She takes Hendricks’ legs from Standard without asking.
“Status?”
“Regulator withdrawal.” Elena’s voice is flat. Efficient. “Systemic shock. Dehydrated.”
They heave him onto a stretcher. The medic checks his pupils. Checks his neck. She sees the scar—fresh, pink, still healing—where the node used to be. She pauses.
She knows what that scar means. She knows what kind of men wear those nodes.
She doesn’t ask.
“Name?” She pulls out a triage tag. Her pen hovers.
“Hendricks.”
“Just Hendricks?”
She remembers the name on the NED credentials. “Uh… Sabino. Sabino Hendricks.”
Standard stands at the edge of the tent. She is holding the flap open. She doesn’t know why. The fabric is heavy and damp and it smells like mildew, and she is holding it, and she hears the name.
Sabino.
Not Swanstrom Kim.
Elena turns. Sees her standing there. Points at the Rover.
“Wait there. Don’t move.”
Her voice is already somewhere else. Thinking about the next thing. Standard is not the next thing.
Standard walks back to the Rover. She sits in the passenger seat. The cold seeps through the borrowed clothes. The smell of old blood and hot metal sits in her chest.
Sabino.
The first words he ever spoke to her. She thought it was his name. She thought he was offering something—a handle, a way to know him. But now there is another name, a real name, and the first one sits in her memory like a stone she swallowed without chewing.
What else did she accept without question?
He came back for her on the stairs. He used bullets he was saving for something. He shook so badly he couldn’t hold a glass, but his hands were steady when he pulled the trigger. She built a structure out of those facts—he is safe, he is mine, I can follow him—and now one of the foundations is cracked.
Maybe the others are too.
She doesn’t know what Swanstrom Kim Hendricks means. She doesn’t know why he said it, or why he never corrected her. She only knows that a man who lies about his name might lie about other things. Might already have.
And Elena lies too. They all lie. Maybe lying is what humans do when they’re afraid, and everyone here is afraid, and Standard is the only one who doesn’t know what to be afraid of yet.
There is nothing holding her to the seat. No voice. No anchor. Elena said wait, but the word is dissolving, losing its shape the longer she sits with it.
She opens the door.
The mud takes her boot. She pulls it free. Takes another step. The mud takes that one too.
She walks because walking feels like something. Because sitting felt like nothing. Because her body wants to move and she has forgotten how to tell it no.
A kiosk. Rusted engine parts. She doesn’t remember walking here. A puddle. Iridescent. Oil-slick. The only wet thing she’s seen in miles. She watches the colors shift and swirl. She doesn’t know how long she watches.
A woman sits by a plastic drum, shotgun across her knees. The drum says POTABLE in faded letters. The woman’s eyes say don’t.
People move around Standard like water around a stone. They are watching the sky. They are huddled over heating units. They are murmuring to each other, words she can’t quite catch, and they are licking their lips. Everyone is licking their cracked lips.
A cluster of men squat around a heating unit near the water station. One is cleaning a rifle. Another is talking, low and fervent, hands moving like he’s shaping something invisible.
“—the Prophet knew. He’s been saying it for years. Connection invites judgment.” He jabs a finger at the black bars overhead. “And now look. Look what the Sky brought down on us.”
“The Sky didn’t bring this.” Another man, older, a twist of blackened metal hanging from his collar like a charm. “The Sky is this. The merged ones. The uploaded. They rang the dinner bell and something answered.”
“Same thing.” The first man spits into the dust. “Point is, Ash was right. Silence is the only sanctuary. The Quiet Zone’s the only place they can’t reach. The only place we will be safe.”
“If he opens the gates.”
“He’ll open them. For the faithful.” The older man touches the charm at his collar. “The Forgiven will inherit. The connected will burn.”
Standard doesn’t know what Forgiven means. Moves on.
She passes a row of stalls. Engine parts laid out on tarps. Ammunition under glass. Water vouchers in a locked case, more precious than any of it.
And at the end of the row, standing in a doorway: a woman.
She isn’t moving. Her hands are folded in front of her. Her eyes are open but unfocused, tracking nothing. She wears a silk slip that was white once, stained now with dust and something darker at the hem. Her feet are bare. Her lips are painted a red that’s cracked and fading.
Standard slows.
The woman has her face.
Or very similar. Not exactly—the hair is different, longer, tangled and unwashed. And there’s a painted beauty mark above her lip, a small dark dot meant to suggest something. History, maybe. Character. But the cheekbones. The jaw. The distance between the eyes.
Standard touches her own face. The same planes under her fingertips.
A man emerges from the dim space behind the woman. He’s buckling his belt. He doesn’t look at her as he passes—just reaches out, taps something at the base of her skull, and keeps walking.
The woman blinks. Shifts. Resumes her position in the doorway, hands folded, waiting. Eyes open and seeing nothing.
Standard watches. Something builds in her chest. Pressure without a name.
Another man approaches the doorway—younger, gray fatigues, the same twisted-metal charm at his collar. He stops in front of the woman, studies her face, then shakes his head.
“Calloway Sunset Vance.”
Three words. Flat. Bored.
The woman stops. Not frozen—empty. The light that wasn’t really there drains out completely. She stands perfectly still, eyes open, a shape that used to move and doesn’t anymore.
Standard’s hand goes to her temple. Pressure. A sound trying to surface.
A name. He’s giving her his name.
Swanstrom Kim Hendricks.
Three words. The same pattern. The same flat delivery. She’d thought—
The man in gray reaches past the frozen woman, retrieves something from inside—a jacket, maybe, or a bottle—and walks away. He doesn’t reset her. She just stands there, head slightly turned, eyes open and seeing nothing.
From the triage tent across the yard, movement. Standard looks.
Hendricks.
He’s propped on the stretcher now, one hand gripping the frame. Conscious. The medic is trying to push him back down, but his eyes aren’t on her. They’re on Standard. On the frozen woman in the doorway. On Standard again.
He saw.
His face does something Standard can’t read. A flicker. A tightening around the eyes. Then he lets the medic push him down, and his gaze slides away, and he’s just a sick old man on a stretcher again.
But he saw.
Standard looks back at the frozen woman. The same face. The same bones. The same stillness underneath, except Standard isn’t still. Standard moves. Standard—
“Hey.”
A hand on her arm.
Standard turns. The man in gray—younger than she thought. Sovereignty fatigues, worn and patched. The twisted-metal charm at his collar. He’s looking at her the way you look at merchandise.
“You lost?” His eyes move down her body. Up again. Assessing. “Your handler dump you?”
Standard doesn’t answer. She doesn’t understand the question.
“Quiet type. Nice.” He steps closer. His hand moves from her arm to her jaw, tilting her face toward the gray light. Studying the planes of it. “Good bone structure. Old model? Kept mint, though.”
His thumb brushes her lower lip.
Something happens in Standard’s chest. A heat. A tightness. Her hand comes up—fast, faster than she meant—and closes around his wrist.
She squeezes.
The man’s face changes. The lazy assessment drains out, replaced by something else. Pain. Confusion.
“The fuck—”
“I’m not lost.” Her voice sounds strange to her own ears. Flat. Cold. “And I don’t have a handler.”
She releases his wrist. He stumbles back, cradling it against his chest. His eyes are different now. Scared.
“Crazy malfunctioning bitch.” He’s backing away. He spits at her feet. “Someone should put you down.” He turns. Walks fast. Doesn’t look back.
Standard stands in the mud. Her hand is shaking. 
She doesn’t know why.
