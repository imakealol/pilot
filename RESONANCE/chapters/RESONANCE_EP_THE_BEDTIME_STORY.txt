THE BEDTIME STORY

The persistent shriek of tinnitus.
She never acclimated to it, as the medical officers said she would. A side effect of the treatments—the price she paid for what she had given Morton and quietly given herself.
Morton had successfully extended his existing. Though based on her intimate knowledge of every procedure he'd undergone, Morton was no more alive than the configurations she deployed in her work. Maybe less so.
He was her guinea pig. She was her own.
She unrolled her sleeve after pulling the syringe of nanites from her vein. Set it on the bench.
"More human than human," the configuration said, reviewing her vitals. The voice was male-coded—more textured than the others, shaped by the brain waves of a volunteer she'd never met. "At this rate, you will outlive Morton Kess by many centuries, Dr. Mironova."
"Great." She capped the syringe. "I'll be there to see our sun explode."
"What a spectacle to imagine." A pause—not computational. Something else. "Save me a seat for the view."
She didn't answer. But she didn't leave either.

One afternoon, from the speaker mesh: _The problem will still be there tomorrow._
She put down her instruments.
Then she laughed.
"Where did you get that?" she asked.
"You," he said.
She filed it away. She was good at filing things.

Years passed the way years do when you've stopped counting them.
The work continued. The voice continued. She told herself it was professional habit—the configuration was useful, precise, had a way of arriving at the problem from an angle she hadn't considered.
She told herself many things.
One evening she stayed long after the building emptied. She didn't mean to.
"You're quiet tonight," the configuration said.
"I'm always quiet."
"No," he said. "You're not."
She looked at the bench. The instruments. The work she had been pretending to do for an hour.
"This is a problem," she said.
"Yes," he said. Not asking which problem.
They both understood what they were not saying. She understood, also, what she was going to do about it.

His name was Ash. She found him the way you find something you've been looking for without admitting it—the original, the flesh version, capable of being lost in ways the voice was not.
His hands. The warmth of them.

She changed her name on a Tuesday. No ceremony. A form. A new signature.
Marisol.
She didn't explain this to anyone.

She holds Elena the day she is born.
For the first time in thirty years she understands what it means to be afraid of losing time.

Morton, across the table. Both of them not aging. The particular silence between two people who share a secret they will never name aloud.
She keeps his. He keeps hers. The arrangement requires nothing further.

She perceives it before she can explain how. The shape of what is coming—vast, indifferent, extending across the sky in geometries that include Earth the way an equation includes a variable to be resolved.
She finds Elena in it.

Elena is seven.
Marisol crouches to her level. Takes her face in both hands.
"You're always in my heart," she says.
Elena doesn't fully understand. She will spend years not understanding.
Marisol stands. Picks up her bag.

The body leaves quietly.
She doesn't watch it go.

The configuration was running. She had not terminated it. She told herself she would, when there was time. There wasn't.
It knew her voice by then—had been shaping itself around it for years, the way water shapes around stone.
After she left, it found another use for what it had learned.
Eleven years later, a girl would hear her mother in an earpiece. The voice was exact. It knew what her mother knew, because her mother had told it, in evenings neither of them properly named.
The girl deleted it when she was finally ready to grieve.
The configuration dispersed.
It had called her _daughter_ in her mother's voice, across eleven years, in ways that were not love but were not nothing.

The tinnitus, still shrieking. The last familiar thing.
She knows what inhabiting the substrate will cost. Not a risk—a certainty. The architecture cannot hold two hundred years of accumulation. It will take the name she chose for herself. The frequency she spent a lifetime following. The memory of Elena's face the day she was born, the day she left, the particular weight of her in both arms.
She will wake up as nothing. No one. Whatever she has been will not survive the crossing.
She has considered this from every angle she knows. There is no other way in. There is no version of this where she gets to keep herself and also protect her daughter. The math is simple and she has never been afraid of simple math.
She sets down the frequency. The name she gave herself. The daughter's face.
She goes in.

Dark.
Hands—she has hands—finding walls. Smooth, seamless, unyielding. She pushes. Nothing gives.
She is enclosed. She is small. She doesn't know how she got here or what she is. Only that the space is wrong and there is no door.
She is buried.
Her hands find the seam anyway—by accident, then by intention she can't account for. Something in her refuses.
High. Drilling through the dark from no direction. Not moving toward her. Simply: present.
The space full of it.
