
CHAPTER 41

THE DAUGHTER

The compound is dying behind her.
She can hear it through the walls—the Template 3s moving floor by floor, guards who haven't surrendered yet making noise about it, the low percussion of evacuation transports lifting off with the quiet ones inside them. Ash's Quiet Zone. Being unquieted.
She doesn't hurry. Her legs are operating on something below conscious will, carrying her toward a thing she's been walking toward for seven years, or eighteen, or all her life, depending on how you count.
The throne room door is open.
Of course it is. He always leaves doors open. He believes in choices.

Her father stands at the window with his back to her.
He doesn't turn when she enters. He knew she was coming. He's known her footsteps since she was three years old.
Outside the window: the compound on fire. The Template 3s moving through the eastern fields in lines, carrying the quiet ones to transports. The evacuation is almost complete. He's watching it and not saying anything.
"You came alone," he says.
"Yes."
He turns. His kintsugi scars catch the light from outside—the gold threading his face and arms, the Faraday cage he built in his own flesh. He has Hendricks' revolver in his hand. Two bullets left. She's known the count since childhood.
He isn't pointing it at her.
She isn't pointing her sidearm at him.
"It's over," she says.
"That's what your mother said." His voice holds no inflection. It's not unkind. That's always been the thing about him. "_It's over, Alejandro._ As if saying so made it true."
She doesn't answer.
Her thumb finds the scar on her left palm. She doesn't notice.
He comes toward her slowly. Not threatening—her father has never needed to rush anything. He knows how to fill rooms. He stops three meters away and looks at her the way he used to when she came home from something she hadn't told him about—not curious, not concerned. Cataloguing. Looking for what broke.
"You carry their mark," he says. "Something in you has changed."
She doesn't deny it.
"They showed me what you built," she says.
"I built this for you."
"I know."
The Black Box hums in the corner. 287.3 Hz underneath everything, a frequency she's been carrying since the Miracle, since she was seventeen and something got into her bones and never left. She used to think it was damage. She doesn't think that anymore.
She has her sidearm. He has the revolver. She's faster. She's always been faster.
Her arm doesn't rise.
He watches her. He understands before she does. His expression doesn't change—there is no triumph in it, no grief, just the particular certainty of a man who has done the math.
He is quiet for a moment. A man who has spent decades filling silence with doctrine, and he has nothing. She can see him looking for the sentence and not finding it.
"You cannot," he says.
"No."
"Because you love me."
"Yes."
He looks at her for a long moment. He already knew. It doesn't require acknowledgment.
His eyes drop to her free hand, which has come up to the base of her skull without her meaning it to. The implant is warm. Has been warm since the factory reset, since the voice stopped sounding like her mother and started sounding like him—the same cadence under the synthesis, the same particular weight on _daughter_. Eleven years of mission briefings. _Good hunting._ In a voice that was never Marisol's.
She's not going to kill her father.
She opens the door.
The deletion command was three days in her pocket. She activates it.
The signal spikes so hard her vision whites out.
Then the implant pushes _back_—and she's fighting something that knows the inside of her skull, that's been living in her nerve pathways long enough to have a map. She tries to hold the deletion. Something holds _her_.
Her arm rises. Or it does. From somewhere behind her own eyes, she thinks she watches it happen. She tries to stop. Something doesn't. Or she can't tell which way the trying goes anymore.
Her feet carry her forward.
She tries to say his name. Her mouth doesn't open.
Ash watches his daughter's face.
He knows that quality. The lights still on but the person walked somewhere else. He built the Softing to do this—not to bodies, he always said, only to the signal—but he knows what a face looks like when the inhabitant has been displaced. He's seen it on hundreds of people. He gave the order that caused it.
He's never seen it on Elena.
He drops the revolver.
The ceramic blade is at his belt—the one he's had longer than the gold, longer than the Zone, longer than any of this—and his hand finds it, and he doesn't want to, and his daughter keeps coming.
It goes in below her ribs.
Her legs stop.
Her hand opens and the sidearm hits the floor.
Whatever had her lets go all at once—not retreating, more like a signal that was already dispersing, fragments flying apart in the static—and she's back in her body and her body is _wrong_, something tearing in the wrong direction, warmth spreading down her side that has nothing to do with temperature.
She's on her knees. She doesn't remember how she got there. Then she's sideways.
Her hand presses against the wound the way her body knows to do before the pain arrives. Keeping herself inside herself.
Ash stands over her. The blade in his hand. His face is—she doesn't have a word for it. He's crying. He isn't making a sound.
He says her name. Once. Low.
She tries to answer.
The implant hums. 287.3 Hz. Something in the frequency that isn't a voice, that lives underneath, the same thing that was always underneath—through the Miracle, through the years, through every moment she thought it was just damage.
_Stay_, it says without saying anything at all.
The dark comes anyway.
