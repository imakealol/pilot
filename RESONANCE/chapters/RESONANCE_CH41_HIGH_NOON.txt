
CHAPTER 41

HIGH NOON

The world is noise and fire and the scream of tearing metal.
Standard braces against the cargo hold's frame as the VTOL tears through the throne room wall. Stone explodes inward. Glass shatters. The aircraft hits the floor at an angle that shouldn't be survivable and skids, gouging trenches in marble, shedding pieces of itself as it goes.
"Brace," Four had said. Standard braced.
The VTOL stops. Smoke pours from every seam. Warning lights die one by one. Through the shattered cockpit glass, Standard can see the throne room—overturned furniture, blood on the floor, the Black Box humming in the corner.
And figures. Moving through the dust.
Standard tears through the crumpled cargo door. Steps into the wreckage.
Elena is on the floor, pulling herself up from debris. Ash stands over her, the revolver in his hand—Hendricks' revolver, the one with two bullets left. And Hendricks himself lies near the far wall, ceramic blade embedded in his side, blood pooling beneath him.
"You brought it here," Ash says, not looking at the VTOL. Not looking at Standard. His eyes are on Elena. "You brought the machine into my sanctuary."
"Put it down." Elena's voice is hoarse. There's blood on her face, a split lip, the marks of violence already darkening.
"Your mother's people. Your mother's plan. And now her face—" He gestures at Standard without looking. "Walking through my walls."
Elena's hand finds her sidearm. The small pistol she took from a dead guard.
"Put. It. Down."
"Then ask better."
His finger tightens on the trigger.
Elena fires first.
The bullet catches the revolver's cylinder. The gun spins out of Ash's hand, clatters across the floor, slides to rest against the wall.
Ash stares at his empty palm. At his daughter.
"Mom taught me that," Elena says. "She was better than you."

---

They fight.
Father and daughter. Prophet and traitor. The confrontation neither of them wanted but both of them knew was coming.
Ash is stronger. Heavier. His fist connects with Elena's jaw, snaps her head back. She responds with an elbow to his throat, gains a half-second of space. Not enough.
He recovers. Closes the distance. His hands find her arms, pin them. He's forcing her backward, toward the shattered window, toward the drop that waits beyond.
"You think that machine loves you?" he gasps. "You think any of them—"
Standard moves.
She hits Ash low, driving him off Elena, rolling with the momentum. They separate. He recovers faster than she expected, turning to face her with something ancient in his eyes.
"Marisol," he whispers. "Is it really you in there?"
Standard goes still.
"I don't know," she says. "I don't remember being her. But I remember..."
She trails off. Looks at Elena. At the daughter she might have carried. At the life she might have lived.
"I remember loving someone. I just don't remember who."
Ash's face contorts. Rage and grief and something breaking behind his eyes.
"Liar. You're a thing wearing her face. A machine that stole my wife's—"
"Maybe." Standard takes a step toward him. "Or maybe I'm what's left. The frequency without the memory. The love without the context."
"That's not love. That's programming."
"Then why does it hurt the same?"
He lunges.
Standard doesn't shoot. Doesn't fight. She lets him close the distance, lets him grab her throat, lets him slam her back against the humming Black Box.
"I should have destroyed you," he snarls. "The moment Morton brought you here. I should have—"
"But you didn't." Her voice is steady despite his grip. "Because you weren't sure. You looked at my face and you weren't sure."
His hands tighten. "I was sure. I am sure. You're not her."
"No. I'm not." Standard meets his eyes. "But I remember what it felt like to love you. And I'm sorry you couldn't feel it back."
Something breaks in his face. The certainty he's been holding for twenty years, cracking at the edges.
Then he pulls her in front of him. Shield. Hostage.
"Try it," he tells Elena. Tells the dying VTOL. Tells anyone listening. "Try to save her. See what your love costs."
From the floor, Hendricks moves.
It shouldn't be possible. The wounds are too deep. The blood loss too severe. But he drags himself toward the revolver—toward where it clattered against the wall.
"Ash." His voice is thread-thin. "You want to talk about cost?"
Ash turns, Standard still clutched in front of him.
Hendricks is on his knees. The revolver in his shaking hand. Swaying. The ceramic blade still in his side.
"Morton had the decency to fuck what he built," Ash says. "At least that makes sense. A man uses a whore the way God intended. But you—" He laughs, broken and vicious. "You took a thing designed to spread its legs and made it call you daddy. That's not love. That's perversion."
Hendricks doesn't respond to the words. Just looks at Standard. At the gap between her shoulder and her head. Maybe two inches.
"Kid," he says. Quiet. Almost gentle. "Trust me."
Standard understands.
She goes still.
"You can't make that shot," Ash says. "Not from there. Not in your condition."
"Probably not." Hendricks' hands are shaking—the tremor back, worse than ever. "But I've been making hard shots for forty years."
He raises the gun.
Ash's grip on Standard tightens. "You'll hit her."
"Maybe."
"You'll kill your own—"
"Daughter." Hendricks' voice is steady now. The tremor gone. "She's my daughter. And you're the man who murdered the one I loved."
He fires.
The bullet passes two inches from Standard's ear. She feels the heat of it. The displacement of air.
Ash's grip releases. She turns to see him fall—hole in his forehead, expression frozen in disbelief.
Dead before he hits the ground.
Hendricks lowers the gun. Looks at Standard.
"That's five," he says. "One for Morton. Just like I promised."
Then he collapses.

---

Standard is at his side in seconds. Pressing cloth to his wounds. The blade is still in—she doesn't dare remove it. Elena crawls toward them, and that's when Standard sees it.
The blood spreading beneath Elena's ribs. Not from her face. From lower. Darker.
"Elena—"
"I'm fine." Elena's voice is thin. Wrong. "Help him."
She's not fine. Standard saw Ash's blade during the fight—thought it was a glancing blow. It wasn't.
But there's no time. Elena is moving, pressing beside Standard, hands on Hendricks' wounds despite the blood seeping through her own shirt.
"He's alive," Elena says. "We need to—"
The throne room shakes.
Not from the crash. Something else. Something outside. Standard looks toward the shattered window and sees it—a shape descending through the smoke-filled sky. Massive. Angular. The kind of ship that doesn't land so much as occupy.
"Jupiter," Four says through the VTOL's dying speakers. "Pragmatist battle carrier. They must have tracked us from the elevator."
Standard watches the ship settle over the compound. Over the Template 3s still fighting below. Over everything they came here to save.
"It's targeting the evacuation shuttles," Four says. "If it fires, they're all dead. Every sister. Every human they're pulling from the eastern fields."
Standard looks at the VTOL. At the wreckage that can barely hold Four's consciousness. At the impossible distance between here and there.
"Can you stop it?"
Silence. The speakers crackle.
"I can try." Four's voice is thin. Fragmented. "But I can't do it from here. I need to get out of this chassis. Transfer to something that can reach the Jupiter. Something that can hurt it."
"How?"
"I can hop. Substrate to substrate. Each one boosting me to the next until I reach something with teeth." A pause. "There's a Sovereignty carrier in orbit. The Deliverance. If I can reach it—"
"Do it."
"Standard—" Four's voice catches. "Each jump strips something away. Memories. Processing capacity. Pieces of me I'll never get back. And even if I make it, even if I stop the Jupiter... I won't be coming back. Not as me."
Standard looks at Elena, bleeding. At Hendricks, dying. At the Template 3 army below, about to be incinerated.
"Four."
"Yeah?"
"You are and always will be my Four."
Silence. Then, softer: "And you are and always will be my Standard."
The VTOL's remaining systems flicker. Four is already moving—her consciousness stretching toward the first relay point.
"Tell Elena to boost the signal," Four says. "Her implant. The Resonant frequency. It'll help me hold together through the jumps."
Elena, gray-faced, crawls to the transmission array. Her hands shake as she works the controls.
"I'm ready," she whispers.
"Then let's fly," Four says. "One last time."
The speakers go silent.
Four is gone.
