CHAPTER 13

GRACE

Elena comes around in the passenger seat.
She doesn’t know where she is. The Rover is stopped. Through the windshield: flat ground, gray-brown scrub, the black bars overhead. No road she recognizes. She doesn’t remember stopping.
She gets out.
The ground is soft under her boots. Darker than the wasteland. She takes three steps and it gets darker still, the soil wet at the edges of a low pool—still water, the color of old pewter, no bigger than a room. The Rover is twenty feet back. A chain runs from the tow hook through the mud to the pool’s edge.
Standard is at the end of the chain.
Face down. Hands still curved around it. The mud has gone up around her wrists, around her cheek. Still.
Elena crosses the distance without deciding to. She drops to her knees at Standard’s side.
Not breathing.
She turns her over. Pulls her face out of the mud. Standard’s eyes are open, fixed on nothing. Still as the pool.
“No.” Elena’s hands find her face, her shoulders. “No, no—“
She looks back at the Rover, at the chain, at the tracks pressed into the darkening soil. Miles. It had to be miles.
Standard’s hands are still in the shape of holding.
Elena pries at the fingers. Can’t. Locked, frozen in the shape of the last thing she did.
“Elena.” Hendricks’ voice. Somewhere behind her. “Elena, we need to—“
“Don’t.”
She doesn’t know how long she sits there.
The pool sits still. The scrub doesn’t move. The world keeps going like nothing happened, like the woman in the mud didn’t drag two tons of steel and flesh across however many miles of wasteland.
She wasn’t supposed to matter.
That’s the thing. That’s the absurd, terrible thing. Standard was a mission asset. A void in human shape. A problem to be managed, a tool to be used, a means to an end Elena doesn’t fully understand.
The tears come without permission.
Elena hasn’t cried in years. Not since she was seven, standing in the doorway of their apartment, watching her mother walk away without looking back. She learned then that crying doesn’t change anything. Doesn’t bring anyone back. Just makes your face wet and your chest hurt.
She’s crying now.
“You stupid—“ The words catch in her throat. “You weren’t supposed to—I didn’t even—“
She didn’t even like her.
The survivors have gathered at the edge of the pool. Elena can feel them watching—the boy, Nessa, the others. They wanted Standard dead twelve hours ago. Threw stones at her. Called her a thing.
Now they’re silent.
Nessa is crying. Tears streaming down a face that was twisted with hate this morning. Her hand keeps going to her chest, pressing against something that hurts.
The boy is at the water’s edge, drinking. He keeps looking back. His eyes are red.
Elena doesn’t understand it either.
She thinks about her mother.
She doesn’t mean to. The thoughts just come—unbidden, unwanted. The way Marisol smelled. The sound of her voice. The last words she said before she walked out the door: _I have to go where you can’t follow. Not yet._
Elena was seven. She didn’t understand.
She doesn’t understand. Eighteen years old and she’s spent eleven of them chasing a ghost, carrying a frequency only she can hear, holding a secret that burns in the base of her skull.
_Mija._
The word surfaces from nowhere. A memory that isn’t hers—or is it? She heard it somewhere. In a dream. In a vision. In Standard’s voice, channeling something she couldn’t possibly know.
_She has your face. But not your frequency._
Elena’s hand goes to her implant scar. The one her mother gave her before she left. The last piece of Marisol she has.
“I never got to ask you,” she whispers. Not to Standard. To someone else. To no one. “I never got to ask if you were sorry.”
The pool sits still.
And something shifts.
Elena feels it before she sees it.
A change in the air. A pressure. The void that always surrounded Standard—the absence Elena learned to navigate around—it’s different now. Not gone. Not filled. Just. . . different.
Standard’s fingers twitch.
Elena freezes. Doesn’t breathe. Tells herself she imagined it.
The fingers twitch again. Loosen. The chain slides free into the mud.
“Standard?”
No response. But Standard’s chest rises. Falls. Rises again.
“Standard.”
Her eyes are still open. Still staring. But something is moving behind them now—a light coming back on in an empty house.
Standard blinks.
“Oh fuck—oh fuck, oh fuck—“ Elena is grabbing her shoulders, shaking her, crying harder now. “Standard? Can you hear me? Standard!”
Standard’s head turns. Slowly. Like she’s remembering how.
Her eyes find Elena’s face.
For a long moment, nothing. Just that stare—the one that always made Elena feel like she was being catalogued, measured, filed away.
Then Standard speaks.
“Who. . .” Her voice is rust and gravel, barely a whisper. “Who are you?”
Elena’s heart stops.
“What?”
“I know you.” Standard’s brow furrows. Real confusion—not the careful blankness she usually wears. “I know your face. But I don’t. . .” She looks around. At the pool. At the mud. At the people gathered at the water’s edge. “Where am I?”
“You dragged us here. You—“ Elena can’t finish. Can’t process. “You don’t remember?”
Standard’s eyes come back to her. Something different in them. Something older. Sadder.
“I remember a checkpoint,” she says slowly. Her hand goes to her chest, pressing against the place where the hum lives. “People falling. The sky going wrong.” Her brow furrows. “And then nothing. Until now.”
She stops. Her hand rises—reaching toward Elena’s face, toward her cheek. Catches herself. Pulls back. Confused.
“There’s something,” she says. “A frequency. I can almost—“ She shakes her head. “It’s gone.”
Hendricks appears at Elena’s shoulder. His face is gray, lips cracked, but he’s upright. His eyes find Standard. Hold. Then drop to the mud.
“Kid,” he says softly. “You back with us?”
Standard looks at him. That careful, cataloguing stare. Then something shifts. Recognition, or something like it.
“You’re Swanstrom Kim Hendricks,” she says. “Just Hendricks.”
Something moves across his face. Gone before Elena can name it.
Behind them, Nessa is sobbing openly. The boy is standing, staring, his mouth open. The man who had his hand on his heart at the Array—Elena never learned his name—has sat down in the mud like his legs gave out.
They don’t understand what they’re feeling.
Neither does Elena.
Standard’s gaze moves past Hendricks. Past Elena. To the people at the water’s edge.
“Who are they?”
Elena’s throat tightens. “From the Array. The waystation. They came with us.”
Standard studies their faces. Nessa. The boy. The others. No recognition. No flinch. No memory of stones or accusations or a mob demanding her blood.
“They look tired,” Standard says. “Are they hurt?”
Nessa makes a sound—half sob, half laugh. She steps forward, then stops. Her hands are shaking.
“You don’t—“ Her voice breaks. “You don’t remember us?”
Standard tilts her head. That familiar gesture. “Should I?”
Nessa covers her mouth. The boy turns away, shoulders heaving. The man in the mud puts his face in his hands.
Twelve hours ago, they threw stones at her. Called her a monster. Demanded she prove she was human.
She dragged them here and doesn’t know their names.
“We—“ Nessa tries again. Can’t finish. Falls to her knees in the mud—not in supplication but in collapse. In the weight of something she can’t name.
Standard pulls away from Elena. Takes a step toward the woman. Then another.
“You’re hurt,” Standard says. She kneels beside her. “Can I help?”
Nessa stares at her. This woman she stoned. This woman she called a monster. This woman who died in the mud and came back without a single memory of what was done to her.
“I—“ Nessa’s voice is barely a sound. “I don’t—“
Standard reaches out. Takes her hand. Holds it.
“It’s okay,” she says. “Whatever it is. It’s okay.”
Nessa breaks.
She folds forward, pressing her forehead to Standard’s shoulder, and Standard holds her. Doesn’t understand why. Just knows it’s what you do.
The boy is watching. The man. The others. All of them watching a woman comfort her own killer.
Elena stands at the pool’s edge.
She doesn’t understand any of it.
Standard looks up at Elena over the woman’s shaking shoulders.
“Did I do something wrong?”
“No.” Elena’s voice is barely a whisper. “No, you didn’t do anything wrong.”
Standard tries to stand. Her legs buckle. Elena catches her, holds her up.
“Easy. Easy. You just—you were gone. For a while.”
“I know.” Standard’s voice is thin, confused. “I don’t know what I am.”
Neither do I, Elena thinks. Neither do I.
But something has changed. Something fundamental. The void is still there—that absence where Standard’s presence should register—but it’s shaped differently now. Fuller. Like a room that used to be empty, now holding furniture Elena can’t see.
“Can you walk?” she asks.
Standard tests her legs. One step. Two. Her hand grips Elena’s arm—not for balance, but for something else. Connection. Confirmation.
“I think so,” she says. “I think I remember how.”
That old-sad something in her eyes.
“Thank you,” Standard says. “For waiting.”
Elena doesn’t know what to say. Doesn’t know what any of this means.
“Come on,” she says instead. “There’s water. And we need to figure out what comes next.”
Standard nods. Lets Elena guide her toward the others, toward the pool, toward whatever waits ahead.
Behind them, the chain lies in the mud where she dropped it.
She doesn’t look back.
