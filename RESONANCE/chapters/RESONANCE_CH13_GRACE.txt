CHAPTER 13

GRACE

Elena doesn't know how long she sits there.
The brook keeps running. The trees keep swaying. The world keeps turning like nothing happened, like the woman on the bridge didn't just drag two tons of steel and flesh across ten miles of wasteland on dead batteries and sheer fucking stubbornness.
Standard's hands are still wrapped around the chain. Elena tried to pry them loose. Couldn't. The fingers are locked, frozen in the shape of holding on.
"Elena." Hendricks' voice, somewhere behind her. "Elena, we need to—"
"Don't."
She doesn't look at him. Can't. If she looks away from Standard's face, it becomes real. If she moves, it's over. As long as she stays here, holding these cold hands, there's still a chance that—
That what? That she'll blink? That she'll sit up and ask another question Elena doesn't know how to answer?
The tears come without permission.
Elena hasn't cried in years. Not since she was seven, standing in the doorway of their apartment, watching her mother walk away without looking back. She learned then that crying doesn't change anything. Doesn't bring anyone back. Just makes your face wet and your chest hurt.
She's crying now.
"You stupid—" The words catch in her throat. "You weren't supposed to—I didn't even—"
She didn't even like her.
That's the thing. That's the absurd, terrible thing. Standard was a mission asset. A void in human shape. A problem to be managed, a tool to be used, a means to an end Elena still doesn't fully understand.
She wasn't supposed to matter.

---

The survivors from the Array are gathered at the edge of the bridge. Elena can feel them watching—the boy, the woman, the others whose names she never learned. They wanted Standard dead twelve hours ago. Threw stones at her. Called her a thing.
Now they're silent.
Nessa is crying. Elena sees it in her peripheral vision. Tears streaming down a face that was twisted with hate this morning. Nessa doesn't seem to understand why. Her hand keeps going to her chest, pressing against something that hurts.
The boy is on his knees by the brook, drinking, but he keeps looking back. His eyes are red.
They wanted her dead. Now they're mourning her.
Elena doesn't understand it either.

---

She thinks about her mother.
She doesn't mean to. The thoughts just come—unbidden, unwanted. The way Marisol smelled. The sound of her voice. The last words she said before she walked out the door: I have to go where you can't follow. Not yet.
Elena was seven. She didn't understand.
She still doesn't understand. Eighteen years old and she's spent eleven of them chasing a ghost, carrying a frequency only she can hear, holding a secret that burns in the base of her skull.
Mija.
The word surfaces from nowhere. A memory that isn't hers—or is it? She heard it somewhere. In a dream. In a vision. In Standard's voice, channeling something she couldn't possibly know.
She has your face. But not your frequency.
Elena's hand goes to her implant scar. The one her mother gave her before she left. The last piece of Marisol she has.
"I never got to ask you," she whispers. Not to Standard. To someone else. To no one. "I never got to ask if you were sorry."
The brook keeps running.
The wind moves through the trees.
And something shifts.

---

Elena feels it before she sees it.
A change in the air. A pressure. The void that always surrounded Standard—the absence Elena learned to navigate around—it's different now. Not gone. Not filled. Just... different.
Standard's fingers twitch.
Elena freezes. Doesn't breathe. Tells herself she imagined it.
The fingers twitch again. Loosen. The chain slides free, clattering against the concrete.
"Standard?"
No response. But Standard's chest rises. Falls. Rises again.
"Standard."
Her eyes are still open. Still staring. But something is moving behind them now—a light coming back on in an empty house.
Standard blinks.
"Oh fuck—oh fuck, oh fuck—" Elena is grabbing her shoulders, shaking her, crying harder now. "Standard? Can you hear me? Standard!"
Standard's head turns. Slowly. Like she's remembering how.
Her eyes find Elena's face.
For a long moment, nothing. Just that stare—the one that always made Elena feel like she was being catalogued, measured, filed away.
Then Standard speaks.
"Who..." Her voice is rust and gravel, barely a whisper. "Who are you?"
Elena's heart stops.
"What?"
"I know you." Standard's brow furrows. Confusion. Real confusion—not the careful blankness she usually wears. "I know your face. But I don't..." She trails off. Looks around. At the bridge. The brook. The trees. The people gathered at the edge, staring. "Where am I?"
"You're—we're at a river. A brook. You dragged us here. You—" Elena can't finish. Can't process. "You don't remember?"
Standard's eyes come back to her. There's something different in them. Something older. Sadder.
"I remember..." She says slowly. Her hand goes to her chest, pressing against the place where the hum lives. "I remember a checkpoint. People falling. The sky going wrong." Her brow furrows. "And then... nothing. Until now."
She stops. Looks at Elena again.
Her hand rises—reaching toward Elena's face, toward her cheek. Standard catches herself. Pulls back. Confused.
"There's something," she says. "A frequency. I can almost—" She shakes her head. "It's gone."

---

Hendricks appears at Elena's shoulder. His face is gray, his lips cracked, but he's upright. His eyes find Standard. Hold. Then drop to the concrete.
"Kid," he says softly. "You back with us?"
Standard looks at him. Studies his face the way she did before—that careful, cataloguing stare. Then something shifts. Recognition, or something like it.
"You're Swanstrom Kim Hendricks," she says. "Just Hendricks."
Something moves across his face. Gone before Elena can name it.
Behind them, Nessa is sobbing openly now. The boy is standing, staring, his mouth open. One of the other survivors—a man Elena never spoke to—has his hand over his heart, pressing hard, like he's trying to hold something in.
They don't understand what they're feeling.
Neither does Elena.
Standard's gaze drifts past Hendricks. Past Elena. To the people gathered at the edge of the bridge.
"Who are they?"
Elena's throat tightens. "They're... from the Array. The waystation. They came with us."
Standard studies their faces. Nessa. The boy. The others. No recognition. No flinch. No memory of stones, of accusations, of a mob demanding her blood.
"They look tired," Standard says. "Are they hurt?"
Nessa makes a sound—half sob, half laugh. She steps forward, then stops. Her hands are shaking.
"You don't—" Her voice breaks. "You don't remember us?"
Standard tilts her head. That familiar gesture, the one that always made Elena feel like she was being scanned. "Should I?"
Nessa covers her mouth. The boy turns away, shoulders heaving. The man with his hand on his heart sits down hard, like his legs have given out.
Twelve hours ago, they threw stones at her. Called her a monster. Demanded she prove she was human.
She dragged them ten miles through hell to save their lives.
And now she doesn't even know their names.
"We—" Nessa tries again. Can't finish. Falls to her knees in the dirt, not in supplication but in collapse. In the weight of something she can't name.
Standard pulls away from Elena. Takes a step toward the woman. Then another.
"You're hurt," Standard says. She kneels in the dirt beside her. "Can I help?"
Nessa stares at her. This woman she stoned. This woman she called a monster. This woman who dragged her to safety and died on a bridge and came back without a single memory of what was done to her.
"I—" Nessa's voice is barely a sound. "I don't—"
Standard reaches out. Takes her hand. Holds it.
"It's okay," she says. "Whatever it is. It's okay."
Nessa breaks.
Not crying now—something deeper. Something that comes from a place beyond tears. She folds forward, pressing her forehead to Standard's shoulder, and Standard holds her. Doesn't understand why. Just knows it's what you do.
The boy is watching. The man. The others. All of them watching a woman comfort her own killer.
Elena stands frozen on the bridge.
She doesn't understand what she's seeing. Doesn't understand any of it.
Standard looks up at Elena over the woman's shaking shoulders.
"Did I do something wrong?"
"No." Elena's voice is barely a whisper. "No, you didn't do anything wrong."
Standard tries to stand. Her legs buckle. Elena catches her, holds her up.
"Easy. Easy. You just—you were gone. For a while. Your power cells—"
"I don't know what that means." Standard's voice is thin, confused. "I don't know what I am."
Neither do I, Elena thinks. Neither do I.
But something has changed. Something fundamental. The void is still there—that absence where Standard's presence should register—but it's shaped differently now. Fuller. Like a room that used to be empty, now holding furniture Elena can't see.
"Can you walk?" she asks.
Standard tests her legs. One step. Two. Her hand grips Elena's arm—not for balance, but for something else. Connection. Confirmation.
"I think so," she says. "I think I remember how."
She looks at Elena again. That old-sad something still in her eyes.
"Thank you," Standard says. "For waiting."
Elena doesn't know what to say. Doesn't know what she did. Doesn't know what any of this means.
"Come on," she says instead. "There's water. And we need to figure out what comes next."
Standard nods. Lets Elena guide her toward the brook, toward the others, toward whatever waits ahead.
Behind them, the chain lies on the bridge where Standard dropped it.
She doesn't look back.
