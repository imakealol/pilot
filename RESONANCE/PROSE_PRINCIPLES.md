# PROSE PRINCIPLES: A DIAGNOSTIC TOOL

## Purpose

This document is a diagnostic tool, not a style guide. Its purpose is to give the reader — human or LLM — a structured set of questions to ask of the prose, with the analysis preceding any judgment about what to change.

Do not read this document and then hunt for violations. Read the chapter first. Then run each diagnostic question against what you found. The analysis comes before the judgment. The judgment comes before the edit.

Examples are drawn from the Resonance manuscript. Positive examples show the principle correctly applied. Negative examples show the failure mode the principle is correcting against, or the over-correction failure mode where the principle is applied where it shouldn't be.

---

## Principle One: The Rhythm Rule

**Stated:** Dialogue tags ("said"), naked dialogue (no tag), and physical beats are rhythm notes, not quality tiers. None is inherently better than the others. The note is selected based on what the exchange needs at that moment. *Said* is the quarter note — it keeps time, disappears into the prose, identifies the speaker. Naked dialogue is the eighth note — faster, lighter, keeps a rally going. Physical beats are half notes — they slow the exchange and ask the reader to sit inside a pause.

**Diagnostic question:** For each line of dialogue, what rhythm note is it playing? For each physical beat or sensory description in or around dialogue, does it change how the reader receives the next line? If yes, it earns its place. If no, it is stage business and should be replaced with the cleaner note (a tag or naked dialogue).

**Do not:**
- Treat *said* as unliterary. *Said* works because it works.
- Strip tags from a long, slow exchange in pursuit of "show don't tell." Tags anchor the speaker and disappear into the prose.
- Insert physical action between every line of dialogue to avoid using *said*. This produces stage business.
- Apply this principle to rapid dialogue exchanges where naked dialogue is already serving the pace.

**Positive example (CH30 Hangar exchange):** The hum between "Said I was like a father" and "I tried to deactivate her" is a physical beat that fills the space where Hendricks' shame is forming. It changes how the next line lands. It earns its place.

**Negative example (CH30 earlier draft):** The line "The cabin waits." between Four's pronoun-question setup and her answer was atmosphere for atmosphere's sake. Cut.

## Principle Two: The Silence Rule

**Stated:** There is no such thing as silence in a scene. Real rooms always have ambient sound. When the prose reaches for "a silence," it is a failure of attention — the writer looked away from what was actually in the room. The ambient sound that was always there is itself a rhythm note available to the writer.

**Diagnostic question:** Every time the chapter uses the word "silence" or names an absence of sound, what ambient noise is actually present in the scene, and is it on the page? Could the ambient noise replace the "silence" beat and do more work?

**Do not:**
- Replace every instance of "silence" mechanically. Some uses of the word are correct — a character notices the absence of an expected sound; a sound has just stopped; the silence is the content of the moment. The diagnostic is whether the writer is using "silence" as a stand-in for prose they didn't write.
- Add ambient noise that doesn't belong to the scene. The fix is to find the sound that was always there, not to invent one.

**Positive example (CH06):** "It is not a quiet silence. It is a vacuum silence — the sudden, violent depressurization of a space that was previously full of noise." This use of "silence" is correct because the silence is what's happening — the prior noise has been violently removed and the violence of the removal is the content of the beat.

**Negative example (CH06 unrevised):** "A silence, the softer kind" between Hendricks' lines about Morton was a placeholder for ambient sound the prose hadn't gone looking for. The VTOL hum was available; the prose hadn't reached for it.

## Principle Three: The Fragment Rule

**Stated:** Fragments isolate; sentences relate. Reach for the form that matches what the line is actually doing.

If the line is naming a thing — a noun, an object, a person, a single fact that will matter — a fragment can isolate it like a label, and the isolation does work the surrounding prose can't.

If the line is making an argument — a relation between things, a conclusion drawn from evidence, a verdict about a situation — it requires a sentence. The connective tissue is what carries the relation. Stripping it out doesn't make the argument heavier; it makes the argument illegible while pretending to be heavier.

The test is not "fragment vs. sentence." It is: what kind of statement is this line making?

**Diagnostic question:** For each fragment in the chapter, what is the line naming — a thing or a relation? If a thing, the fragment may be earning its form. If a relation, the line wants a sentence. Also check the over-correction failure mode: is a complete sentence being forced into fragment form to perform impact it hasn't earned?

**Do not:**
- Convert all fragments to sentences. Many fragments earn their form — lists of objects, accounting beats, dialogue rhythms, single-noun isolations.
- Strip fragments that are isolating load-bearing nouns. *And the revolver.* on its own line is the chapter announcing a load-bearing object; the fragment is correct.
- Force verdicts or conclusions into fragment form. Verdicts have subjects and predicates because they're making arguments; fragmenting them flattens the argument into list items that don't reassemble in the reader's head.
- Use fragments as a default contemporary literary register. Fragments performing urgency rather than embodying it are the failure mode.

**Positive example (CH03):** *And the revolver.* on its own line. The fragment isolates a load-bearing noun that carries the book's central arithmetic across forty-six chapters. The form (isolation) and the content (one thing that will matter) match.

**Positive example (CH03):** *Five rounds. There used to be six.* Hendricks doing accounting on the cylinder. The fragments are the counting — content as form.

**Negative example (CH07 unrevised):** *Someone that killed everyone whose seat he was bleeding on.* This was a verdict — a relational claim with a verb and a clause and folded irony — forced into fragment form. The argument required connective tissue; the fragmentation flattened it. Revised to: *The architect of those empty seats had been bleeding on one of them since the queue.* — a complete sentence, because the line is an argument.

## Principle Four: The Show-Don't-Tell Micro Rule

**Stated:** Do not name the emotion the physical detail is already carrying. If a physical action, gesture, or environmental fact is already conveying the emotion, the emotional descriptor is doubling work the prose has already done.

**Diagnostic question:** For each emotional descriptor — adjectives like "fearful," "tense," "exhausted"; adverbs like "nervously," "sadly"; narrator labels like "her chest tightened with grief" — is the surrounding physical action already conveying that emotion? If yes, cut the descriptor. If no, find the physical action that should be there instead.

**Do not:**
- Cut descriptors when no physical action is doing the work. The alternative to telling is showing, not silence.
- Cut narrator-labeled emotion that is operating in a register the character cannot self-narrate — a dissociated character's affect, a grief too large to name in the moment. The principle assumes a POV that can be expected to register its own physical state.
- Apply this to dialogue tags. *He said angrily* is a separate problem from interior emotion-labeling.

**Positive example (CH30 model line):** "The words come out rough while he's rubbing the stubble of his cheek." The roughness of the voice and the hand on the stubble convey hesitation, exhaustion, and the difficulty of saying what he's about to say. No emotional label is needed.

**Negative example (CH06 unrevised):** "The word flat. Final." after Four's "Yes." The single-word *Yes* with a period is already flat and final on the page. Naming the flatness diminishes it.

## Principle Five: The Affirmative Rule

**Stated:** Lead with the affirmative. Let negation do work only when the negative is the content. When the prose says what something isn't, doesn't, or doesn't have before saying what it is, does, or has, the negation is usually throat-clearing — the writer warming up to the affirmative. The affirmative is the line.

**Diagnostic question:** When the prose reaches for *not-X*, *isn't-X*, *doesn't-X*, ask whether the affirmative version of the same content would be cleaner. Is the negative carrying the content — the character is registering an absence; the not-doing is what's happening — or is the negative warming up to the affirmative that follows?

**Do not:**
- Cut all negatives. *He doesn't answer* is correct when the not-answering is the beat. *They don't follow* is correct when the not-following is the protective fact.
- Convert negation to affirmative when the negation is registering an absence the character is noticing. Absence-noticing is content.

**Positive example (CH04):** *He doesn't answer.* — Hendricks not responding to Standard's questions in the stairwell. The not-answering is the content; the silence between them is the relationship at that stage.

**Negative example (CH07 unrevised):** *It isn't a town. It is refuse that forgot to scatter.* The first sentence is the writer warming up before the affirmative. *Refuse that forgot to scatter* is the line.

**Negative example (CH07 unrevised):** *Standard doesn't know. She has no way of knowing.* The first asserts; the second softens the assertion with epistemological framing. Pick one. *Standard has no way of knowing* is sharper because the reason is inside the assertion.

## How to Use This Document

**Workflow:**

1. Read the chapter. Do not consult this document during the read.
2. After the read, run each diagnostic question against what you found.
   - Rhythm Rule: scan dialogue exchanges for stage business — physical beats that don't change how the next line lands.
   - Silence Rule: search the text for "silence" and absence-of-sound constructions.
   - Fragment Rule: identify every sentence fragment. For each, ask: thing or relation?
   - Show-Don't-Tell: identify emotional descriptors alongside physical action.
   - Affirmative Rule: search for *not-X*, *isn't-X*, *doesn't-X* constructions. For each, ask: is the negative the content, or is it throat-clearing?
3. For each candidate flagged, check the **do-not addendum** before proposing a revision. Confirm the principle applies. Confirm the over-correction failure mode is not present.
4. Only after steps 2 and 3 propose the revision.

**Important:** The diagnostic questions will produce false positives. A "silence" that is correct, a fragment that earns its form, a physical beat that changes how the next line lands — these are not problems. The do-not addendums exist because over-application is as much a failure mode as under-application. Measure twice.
